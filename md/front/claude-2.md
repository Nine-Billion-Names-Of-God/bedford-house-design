# Front Garden Planting Design
## Victorian Terraced House — Bedford Park, Chiswick

---

## 1. Summary of Key Constraints

| Constraint | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Orientation** | West-facing — morning shade, strong afternoon/evening sun |
| **Soil** | London clay (heavy, moisture-retentive, alkaline-neutral) |
| **Area character** | Bedford Park conservation area — traditional London front garden feel |
| **Style** | Structured but natural — neither formal parterre nor loose cottage |
| **Garden dimensions** | 4.0 m wide × 4.6 m deep (3.9 m at bay window) |
| **Planting bed depth** | 70–80 cm throughout |
| **Height limits** | Most planting ≤ 1.5 m; up to 2.0 m at the front boundary only |
| **Privacy** | Evergreen layered screening along front boundary — airy, not hedge-like |
| **Bike access** | ≥ 1.0 m clearance in front of shed; doors must open fully |
| **Tap access** | Right side of bay window — must remain unobstructed |
| **Fixed elements** | Path, gate, shed, front wall/fence, hedges, house façade — no changes |
| **Central court** | Open shingle retained for circulation; optional focal plant if compact |
| **Winter interest** | Evergreen backbone required; garden must not look empty in winter |
| **Maintenance** | Moderate — regular pruning and seasonal tidying acceptable |

---

## 2. Overall Planting Structure

The planting strategy follows the brief's **layered boundary planting with open central shingle court** approach. The structure works in three tiers:

### Spatial Organisation

```
    HOUSE WALL
    ┌───────────────────────────────────┐
    │  Zone 2: House Wall Bed (70-80cm) │  ← Climbers, wall shrubs, perennials
    │  [bay window]  [tap]              │
    ├───────────────────────────────────┤
    │                                   │
    │     Zone 4: Central Shingle       │  ← Open court, optional focal plant
    │     Court (~2.4–2.6 m wide)       │
    │              [SHED]───────┐       │
    │                           │       │
    ├───────────────────────────┤       │
    │  Zone 3: Front Boundary Bed       │  ← Privacy screening, evergreen + flowering
    │  (70–80cm deep)                   │
    └───────────────────────────────────┘
    FRONT BOUNDARY (brick wall + timber fence)

    Zone 1: Left Hedge Strip (1.0 m × 4.6 m) runs alongside the path
```

### Three-Tier Height Strategy

| Tier | Height | Location | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Upper** | 150–200 cm | Back of Zone 3 (against fence) | Privacy screening, evergreen structure |
| **Middle** | 60–120 cm | Front of Zone 3, Zone 2 | Flowering interest, seasonal colour |
| **Lower** | 20–60 cm | Bed edges, Zone 1, Zone 2 front | Ground cover, edging, textural contrast |

### Planting Principles

1. **Evergreen backbone** — at least 60% of structural planting is evergreen for winter presence
2. **Layered depth** — even in 70–80 cm beds, arrange back-to-front by height
3. **Seasonal relay** — winter fragrance → spring blossom → summer flowers → autumn foliage
4. **Repeated rhythm** — use 2–3 plants in drifts rather than one of everything
5. **Appropriate scale** — nothing that will outgrow the space within 5 years

## 3. Zone-by-Zone Planting Recommendations

### Zone 1 — Left Hedge Strip (1.0 m × 4.6 m)

**Character:** Narrow strip beneath/beside the existing hedge. Partially shaded by the hedge itself and by the house in the morning. Receives afternoon sun filtering through/over the hedge.

**Design intent:** Improve the bare hedge base with a layered understorey. Low maintenance, shade-tolerant, primarily foliage-based with seasonal highlights.

| Layer | Role | Height | Suitable plant types |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hedge base | Soften bare stems | 30–60 cm | Shade-tolerant evergreen ground cover |
| Seasonal accent | Spring/winter interest | 15–40 cm | Bulbs, early perennials |
| Textural filler | Year-round foliage | 30–50 cm | Ferns, heuchera |

**Key considerations:**
- Only 1.0 m wide — plants must stay narrow and not encroach on the path
- Hedge roots will compete for moisture — choose robust, tolerant plants
- Primarily viewed from the path — arrange to face that direction

---

### Zone 2 — House Wall Bed (~70–80 cm deep)

**Character:** Runs along the house wall in the main garden area. The brick wall stores warmth and provides shelter — effectively one microclimate zone warmer than open garden. West-facing, receiving strong afternoon and evening sun.

**Design intent:** Soften the house façade with a climber on trellis (near the door or on available wall space), wall shrubs, and a perennial understorey. The bed wraps around the bay window projection.

| Section | Approx. length | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Left of bay window (near door) | ~0.8–1.0 m | Narrow; climber/trellis position; door clearance needed |
| Bay window face | ~1.5–2.0 m | Very shallow (bay projects 0.7 m); pot plants or very narrow planting only |
| Right of bay window | ~1.5–2.0 m | Best planting area; tap access needed at left end of this section |

**Key considerations:**
- Climber position: ideally to the right of the door or on the wall section right of the bay window
- Tap on right side of bay window — keep a 40–50 cm clear zone around it
- The warm wall microclimate allows slightly tender plants (e.g., Trachelospermum, Daphne)
- Keep planting below window sill height (~1.0–1.2 m) to avoid blocking light

---

### Zone 3 — Front Boundary Bed (~70–80 cm deep, ~2.5 m effective planting length)

**Character:** The most important planting zone. Runs along the front boundary wall and fence. Full afternoon sun. This is the public-facing display and the privacy screen.

**Design intent:** Layered evergreen structure at the back (against the fence) rising to ~1.5–2.0 m, with flowering shrubs and perennials in front stepping down to ~0.6–1.0 m. Provides soft, airy screening — not an opaque wall.

| Layer | Position | Height | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Back (against fence) | 0–30 cm from fence | 150–200 cm | Evergreen screening — Viburnum tinus, Pittosporum, or Osmanthus |
| Middle | 30–50 cm from fence | 80–120 cm | Flowering shrubs — hydrangea, choisya, or roses |
| Front edge | 50–80 cm from fence | 20–50 cm | Low perennials, edging — lavender, geranium, or similar |

**Key considerations:**
- Effective planting length is ~2.5 m (from path to where the shed zone begins)
- There may be room for a narrow low planting strip (20–30 cm) between the shed and the front boundary (~0.7 m depth), but access for maintenance is limited
- The fence is semi-open (Bedford Park style timber), so planting is seen from the street — it should look attractive from both sides
- Avoid plants that will push forward into the central court and obstruct bike manoeuvring

---

### Zone 4 — Central Shingle Court (~2.4–2.6 m wide)

**Character:** Open shingle area serving as the functional heart of the garden. Provides bike access to the shed, general circulation, and visual breathing space.

**Design intent:** Mostly open. An optional single focal plant may be placed centrally, provided it does not obstruct bike access. The shingle can be softened at edges where beds meet the court.

**Key considerations:**
- Minimum 1.0 m clear path between any planting and the shed doors
- Focal plant, if used, should be narrow at base (≤ 0.8 m ground footprint)
- Shingle-to-bed transitions can be softened with low plants that spill slightly over the edge
- No tall planting in this zone — the focal plant is the only exception and should remain under 2.0–2.5 m

## 9. Central Focal Plant Options

The central shingle court may include a single specimen plant as a focal point. This plant must be:
- Compact enough for the ~2.4–2.6 m wide court
- Narrow at the base (≤ 0.8 m footprint) to preserve bike circulation
- Visually striking with preferably flowering interest
- Not a large tree in maturity
- Low to moderate maintenance

### Option 1: Prunus incisa 'Kojo-no-mai' (Fuji Cherry)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mature size** | 1.5–2.0 m H × 1.0–1.5 m W |
| **Habit** | Compact, twisting branches; naturally architectural |
| **Flowers** | Pale pink buds opening white, March–April |
| **Autumn colour** | Orange-red foliage |
| **Winter interest** | Excellent — twisted bare stems create a sculptural silhouette |
| **Maintenance** | Minimal pruning; occasional shaping. Very slow growing |
| **Why suitable** | Perfect scale for a small front garden. Four-season interest (spring blossom, summer foliage, autumn colour, winter structure). Narrow enough for bike access. Quintessentially appropriate for a traditional London garden. Grows well in clay. |

**Recommended — best overall choice for this garden.**

### Option 2: Magnolia stellata (Star Magnolia)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mature size** | 2.0–2.5 m H × 2.0–2.5 m W (after 10–15 years) |
| **Habit** | Rounded, densely branched; slow growing |
| **Flowers** | Starry white, fragrant, March–April (before leaves) |
| **Autumn colour** | Soft yellow |
| **Winter interest** | Furry silver buds on bare branches |
| **Maintenance** | Virtually none — no pruning required. Slow growing |
| **Why suitable** | Spectacular spring flowering. Classic front garden specimen. Well suited to London clay if not waterlogged. Slow growth means it won't outgrow the space quickly. |

**Caveat:** Will eventually spread to 2.0–2.5 m wide, which may begin to affect bike circulation after 8–10 years. Choose this if you accept eventual light pruning to contain width, or are comfortable with a slightly wider footprint.

### Option 3: Amelanchier lamarckii (Snowy Mespilus — multi-stem)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mature size** | 2.5–3.0 m H × 1.5–2.0 m W (as multi-stem; can be raised to clear at base) |
| **Habit** | Open, airy; multi-stem with canopy that can be lifted above head height |
| **Flowers** | White blossom, April (coincides with bronze new foliage) |
| **Berries** | Small dark berries, June–July (birds love them) |
| **Autumn colour** | Spectacular — orange, red, crimson |
| **Winter interest** | Elegant bare branch structure |
| **Maintenance** | Light pruning to remove lower branches and maintain clear stem. Annual light thin |
| **Why suitable** | Multi-season interest. If lower branches are raised to ~1.2 m, bikes can be wheeled underneath. Very common in London front gardens. Tolerates clay well. Gives a sense of a tree without the bulk. |

**Caveat:** Taller than other options — requires annual pruning to maintain scale. May feel slightly large for this garden within 5–7 years, but the airy habit prevents it from feeling heavy.

### Option 4: Hydrangea paniculata 'Limelight' (standard form on clear stem)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mature size** | 1.5–2.0 m H × 1.0–1.2 m W (on standard clear stem ~0.6–0.8 m) |
| **Habit** | Trained as a standard (lollipop); clear stem below, rounded head above |
| **Flowers** | Very large lime-green panicles maturing to white then pink, July–September |
| **Autumn colour** | Dried flower heads persist into winter; golden foliage |
| **Winter interest** | Moderate — dried heads and bare stem; less dramatic than Options 1–3 |
| **Maintenance** | Annual hard prune in March (cut back to 2–3 buds). Clear stem below. Moderate |
| **Why suitable** | Spectacular long summer flowering season. The clear-stem standard form keeps the base completely open for bike access — nothing at ground level. Hydrangeas are locally common in Bedford Park. Strong visual impact from the street. |

**Caveat:** Less interesting in winter than the other options. Requires annual pruning. Needs a sturdy stake permanently. Best if you prioritise summer flower impact over year-round structure.

## 12. Planting Scheme C — "Evergreen Elegance"

**Character:** Maximum year-round structure and presence. A higher proportion of evergreens creating a green, sculpted, somewhat formal feel softened by carefully chosen flowering accents. Ideal for someone who wants the garden to look good every single day of the year with minimal seasonal variation. Japanese-influenced subtlety within a London context.

**Colour palette:** Greens (multiple shades), white, cream, touches of pink/purple

**Seasonal highlight:** Year-round consistency; best quiet moments in February (Sarcococca + Hellebores) and May (Choisya + Clematis)

## 15. Final Recommended Planting Scheme

### Recommendation: **Scheme A — "Classic Bedford Park"**

**Rationale:**

1. **Most appropriate to context** — Bedford Park is an Arts & Crafts conservation area. Roses, hydrangeas, lavender, and Viburnum tinus are the signature plants of Chiswick front gardens. This scheme will look completely at home.

2. **Strongest seasonal fragrance** — The Daphne (Feb–Mar), climbing rose (Jun–Sep), and lavender (Jun–Aug) provide scent from the front door path for much of the year.

3. **Best balance of structure and flowers** — The Viburnum tinus provides the evergreen privacy screen, while the roses and hydrangea deliver the summer colour that transforms the garden from barren shingle to planted front garden.

4. **Proven plants on London clay** — Every plant in Scheme A is widely grown on London clay and well-tested in west-facing front gardens throughout Chiswick and Bedford Park.

5. **Moderate maintenance** — Requires seasonal attention (rose pruning in February, lavender trim in August, hydrangea deadheading) but nothing specialist.

6. **Prunus 'Kojo-no-mai' as focal plant** — This compact Fuji cherry is the perfect centrepiece: spectacular in spring, architectural in winter, never too large, utterly appropriate.

## Appendix: Implementation Notes

| Topic | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Soil preparation** | London clay benefits from generous organic matter (composted bark or garden compost) mixed into planting holes. Improve drainage by adding grit to individual planting holes rather than attempting to amend the entire bed |
| **Bed edging** | A clean edge between shingle court and planting beds is essential for the structured-but-natural look. Use a metal or stone edging strip flush with the shingle surface |
| **Mulching** | Mulch all beds with 5–8 cm composted bark after planting to suppress weeds and retain moisture. Top up annually |
| **Climber support** | Install a trellis or horizontal wire system on the house wall before planting the climber. Ensure fixings allow a 3–5 cm gap between trellis and brickwork for air circulation |
| **Planting time** | Best planted October–March (bare-root roses and shrubs) or September–October (evergreens). Container-grown plants can go in year-round but establish best in autumn |
| **Watering** | Water new plantings regularly for the first two summers (especially against the house wall, which can be very dry). Once established, London clay retains moisture well and mature plants should need little supplementary watering |
| **Shed surround** | The 0.5 m strip between the shed and right boundary is too narrow for meaningful planting. Leave as shingle or add a single pot plant for interest |
