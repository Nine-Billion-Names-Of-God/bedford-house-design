# Front Garden Planting Design
### Victorian Terraced House — Bedford Park, Chiswick

---

## 1. Summary of Key Constraints

**Site geometry:** 6.0 m frontage total. Design area is 4.0 m wide × 4.6 m deep (reducing to 3.9 m at the bay window). The 1.0 m left hedge strip and 1.0 m paved path are fixed.

**Orientation:** West-facing — morning shade, strong afternoon and evening sun. Suits a wide range of plants but rules out deep shade specialists.

**Soil:** London clay — moisture-retentive, heavy, fertile when improved. Favours moisture-tolerant plants; avoid drought-intolerant species without amendment.

**Fixed elements:** Path, gate (opens inward, swings left), bike shed (1.0 × 2.0 m, 0.5 m from right boundary, doors opening toward garden), front boundary wall and Bedford Park fence, neighbour hedge (right), water tap (right of bay window).

**Critical clearances:** 1.0 m minimum in front of shed doors for bike manoeuvring. Tap must remain accessible. Gate swing must remain unobstructed.

**Height limits:** Most planting under 1.5 m; up to 2.0 m only near the front boundary fence. Bay window projection of 0.7 m must be respected in Zone 2.

**Style:** Structured but natural. Traditional Bedford Park character. Not formal, not cottage-wild.

**Privacy:** Evergreen but airy — layered, not hedge-like, along front boundary (Zone 3).

---

## 2. Overall Planting Structure

The garden is organised around four distinct zones surrounding an open shingle court. The planting strategy builds in three height layers throughout:

**Upper layer (1.5–2.0 m):** Reserved for Zone 3 (front boundary) only — evergreen and flowering shrubs providing partial privacy screening.

**Middle layer (0.6–1.2 m):** The primary flowering and structural layer across all zones — shrubs, larger perennials, and climbers trained on the house wall.

**Lower layer (0.2–0.6 m):** Edging, ground cover, and underplanting throughout — provides year-round texture and suppresses weeds in the shingle margins.

The left hedge strip (Zone 1) is treated as a narrow vertical accent bed with climbers or trained shrubs to complement the existing hedge. The house wall bed (Zone 2) uses a climber as the backbone, with lower perennials and bulbs in front. The front boundary bed (Zone 3) carries the heaviest planting — the privacy and structural work of the garden. The central court (Zone 4) remains open shingle with an optional single focal plant.

---

## 3. Zone-by-Zone Planting Notes

### Zone 1 — Left Hedge Strip (1.0 m × 4.6 m)
A narrow strip running the full depth of the garden alongside the existing hedge. Because hedge roots will compete strongly for moisture and nutrients, plants here must be tolerant of dry shade (at least in the hedge's shadow) and robust enough to hold their own. The strip is also partially shaded by the hedge itself. Best approach: a low-maintenance underplanting of shade-tolerant ground cover with one or two upright accent plants toward the front (boundary end) where light improves. Avoid large shrubs that will compete with the hedge or crowd the path.

### Zone 2 — House Wall Bed (depth ~70–80 cm, full width of house frontage minus bay)
The warmest, most sheltered spot. The brick wall stores heat, creating a favourable microclimate. A climbing plant trained on a trellis or wires is the natural backbone here, with lower perennials and seasonal bulbs in front. The bay window projection limits depth on the right side. The tap (right of bay) must remain accessible — keep planting away from it or use low ground cover only in that corner. The front door area is a key focal point — planting should frame rather than obscure the door.

### Zone 3 — Front Boundary Bed (depth ~70–80 cm, full 4.0 m width)
The most important privacy and visual zone. This bed sits against the front wall and Bedford Park fence. The shed begins immediately behind this bed on the right — planting must not obstruct shed door access (1.0 m clearance maintained in front of shed doors). Planting here should be evergreen-dominant at the back, with flowering shrubs and perennials stepping down toward the shingle court. Heights can reach 2.0 m at the fence, reducing to ~1.0–1.2 m at the front edge of the bed.

### Zone 4 — Central Shingle Court (~2.4–2.6 m open width)
Retained as open shingle. Primary purpose is bike access and visual openness. An optional single focal plant can be positioned toward the centre-front of the court (away from the shed clearance zone), provided it remains compact and does not obstruct bike movement. No spreading or suckering plants.

## 8. Central Focal Plant Options

The optional focal plant sits within the open shingle court. It must be compact, non-suckering, slow-growing, visually striking, and must not impede bike access. Position recommended: toward the front-centre of the court, offset slightly away from the shed clearance zone.

### Option A: *Acer palmatum* 'Garnet' (Japanese Maple)
**Mature size:** 1.5–2.0 m tall × 1.5 m wide after many years; grows very slowly (5–10 cm/year)
**Form:** Weeping, deeply dissected burgundy foliage; spectacular autumn colour
**Flowers:** Small, inconspicuous
**Maintenance:** Very low once established; mulch well; avoid waterlogging (raise in shingle for drainage)
**Why suitable:** Compact, refined, utterly distinctive; ideal for small London gardens; non-invasive; widely used in Bedford Park settings; suits west-facing position with afternoon sun
**Caveat:** Stake well initially; protect from late frost in first years

### Option B: *Hydrangea paniculata* 'Bobo' (Dwarf Panicle Hydrangea)
**Mature size:** 0.7–1.0 m tall × 0.8–1.0 m wide
**Form:** Dense, mounded; large white panicle flowers fading to pink-cream, August–October; attractive dried heads through winter
**Flowers:** Excellent — long season of interest
**Maintenance:** Low; cut back hard in late February to maintain compact form
**Why suitable:** Genuinely dwarf (unlike most panicle hydrangeas); strong visual impact; fits Bedford Park planting tradition; suits London clay and west-facing aspect; non-invasive

### Option C: *Viburnum plicatum* f. *tomentosum* 'Watanabe'
**Mature size:** 1.0–1.5 m tall × 1.2 m wide; very slow-growing
**Form:** Horizontal tiered branching; white lacecap flowers May–June; red berries; good autumn colour
**Flowers:** Very good — architectural form with attractive flowers
**Maintenance:** Low; rarely needs pruning; remove spent wood only
**Why suitable:** Architectural presence without becoming a tree; tiered form is beautiful and distinctive; suits clay soil; elegant and traditional; works beautifully rising from shingle

### Option D: *Daphne × transatlantica* 'Eternal Fragrance'
**Mature size:** 0.8–1.0 m tall × 0.8 m wide
**Form:** Upright, bushy, semi-evergreen; pink-white flowers with intense fragrance, April–October (repeat-flowering)
**Flowers:** Exceptional — almost continuous from spring to autumn
**Maintenance:** Low; dislikes root disturbance; plant in well-drained shingle with organic matter beneath
**Why suitable:** Extraordinary fragrance near the main circulation route; long season; compact; non-invasive; a prestige plant that suits a Bedford Park garden
**Caveat:** Prefers slightly better-drained conditions — position in shingle aids this; all parts toxic if ingested

## 14. Recommended Planting Scheme

**Recommended: Scheme A — *Classic Bedford Park*, with the *Daphne* 'Eternal Fragrance' as the focal plant and fragrance elements borrowed from Scheme C.**

**Reasoning:**

Scheme A provides the most confident, contextually appropriate planting palette for a Bedford Park conservation area front garden. Its evergreen backbone (*Viburnum tinus*, *Choisya*) ensures strong year-round structure, while the flowering layer (*Philadelphus*, *Hydrangea*, *Clematis*) delivers generous seasonal impact without demanding high maintenance. The palette is recognisably at home in Chiswick — this is not a pastiche but a well-considered interpretation of what makes these front gardens so appealing.

The one recommended refinement is to **substitute the Viburnum plicatum focal plant for the *Daphne × transatlantica* 'Eternal Fragrance'** — placing this extraordinary plant in the shingle court where its fragrance will greet visitors approaching from the gate. This single element elevates the garden without complicating the planting scheme.

If the owners want a slightly more contemporary feel in future years, Scheme B elements (particularly the *Pittosporum* and ornamental grasses) can be introduced as simple substitutions in Zone 3 without redesigning the garden.
