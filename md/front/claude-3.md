# Front Garden Planting Design

## Victorian Terraced House — Bedford Park, Chiswick

---

## 1. Summary of Key Constraints

| Constraint | Detail |
|---|---|
| Orientation | West-facing — morning shade, strong afternoon/evening sun |
| Soil | London clay (heavy, moisture-retentive, slightly alkaline) |
| Total frontage | 6.0 m (1.0 m hedge strip + 1.0 m path + 4.0 m garden) |
| Garden depth | 4.6 m max; 3.9 m at bay window (0.7 m projection) |
| Planting bed depth | 70–80 cm throughout |
| Central court width | ≈ 2.4–2.6 m open shingle — must remain clear |
| Bike shed | 1.0 m × 2.0 m; doors open toward garden; 1.0 m clearance required |
| Tap | House wall, right of bay window — access unobstructed |
| Privacy | Evergreen, layered, airy — NOT a hedge wall |
| Height limits | Most planting ≤ 1.5 m; boundary planting up to 2.0 m max |
| Style | Structured but natural; Bedford Park character |
| Maintenance | Moderate acceptable |
| Winter appearance | Must not feel empty — evergreen framework required |
| Hydrangeas | Acceptable but not the sole structural plant |

**Fixed elements (do not move):** path, hedges, gate, front wall/fence, house façade, bike shed and platform, bay window, water tap.

**Layout strategy (do not change):** Layered boundary planting with open central shingle court.

---

## 2. Planting Structure for the Whole Garden

The design uses a **perimeter planting / open centre** model — a green frame of beds surrounding an open shingle court.

### Height Layering (front boundary to centre)

```
FRONT BOUNDARY (fence)
  │
  ├── Back row:  Tall evergreens for privacy    1.5–2.0 m
  ├── Mid row:   Flowering shrubs               0.8–1.2 m
  ├── Front row: Perennials + edging            0.2–0.6 m
  │
  ╔══════════════════════════════╗
  ║   CENTRAL SHINGLE COURT     ║  Open — bikes, circulation
  ╚══════════════════════════════╝
  │
  ├── Front row: Perennials + edging            0.2–0.6 m
  ├── Mid row:   Medium shrubs                  0.6–1.0 m
  ├── Back row:  Wall climber on trellis        2.0–3.0 m
  │
HOUSE WALL
```

### Functional Layers

| Layer | Location | Height Range | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Boundary structure** | Zone 3 back row | 1.5–2.0 m | Evergreen privacy screening |
| **Flowering mid-layer** | Zone 3 mid row | 0.8–1.2 m | Seasonal colour, softening |
| **House wall planting** | Zone 2 | 0.6–1.5 m + climber | Soften façade, frame door |
| **Ground/edging layer** | All zones front | 0.2–0.5 m | Knit beds together, seasonal detail |
| **Hedge underplanting** | Zone 1 | 0.1–0.4 m | Improve bare hedge base |
| **Vertical layer** | Zone 2 trellis | 2.0–3.0 m | Height, interest from street |
| **Focal specimen** | Zone 4 | 1.5–2.5 m | Central visual anchor (optional) |

## 3. Zone-by-Zone Planting Recommendations

### Zone 1 — Left Hedge Strip (1.0 m × 4.6 m ≈ 4.6 sq m)

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Light** | Predominantly shaded — hedge canopy above, house wall behind; some dappled afternoon light |
| **Soil** | Dry shade at hedge base; clay with root competition |
| **Purpose** | Improve bare hedge base; add seasonal colour and texture |
| **Height limit** | 20–40 cm (must stay below hedge canopy) |
| **Bed depth** | 1.0 m (full strip width) |

**Planting approach:** This is a dry-shade strip dominated by hedge roots. Select tough, shade-tolerant ground covers with seasonal highlights from bulbs. The strip is visible primarily from the path, so plant interest should face inward.

**Suitable plant types:**
- Evergreen ground cover forming a continuous carpet (front half nearest path)
- Shade-tolerant perennials with good foliage (mid strip)
- Naturalising bulbs planted through the ground cover (winter/spring interest)

**Key considerations:**
- Avoid anything needing full sun
- Avoid vigorous plants that would compete with the hedge
- Drought tolerance important (hedge roots take moisture)

---

### Zone 2 — House Wall Bed (≈ 0.75 m deep × ≈ 3.5 m long effective ≈ 2.6 sq m)

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Light** | Morning shade from house; strong afternoon/evening sun reflected from brick wall |
| **Soil** | Dry at wall base (rain shadow); clay beneath. Will need soil improvement and watering in first year |
| **Purpose** | Climber on trellis near front door; soften house wall; frame bay window |
| **Height limit** | Climber 2.0–3.0 m on trellis; shrubs 0.6–1.2 m |
| **Constraints** | Bay window projects 0.7 m; tap access right of bay must stay clear |

**Sub-zones:**

| Sub-zone | Location | Width | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **2a** | Left of bay window (near door) | ≈ 1.0–1.2 m | Climber on trellis + low underplanting |
| **2b** | Right of bay window to shed zone | ≈ 2.0–2.3 m | Medium shrub + perennials; keep tap accessible |

**Planting approach:**
- **Zone 2a:** Single climber on a trellis panel mounted to the left of the front door. This is the primary vertical element seen from the street. Low perennials beneath.
- **Zone 2b:** One medium evergreen shrub positioned to the right of the bay window but set back enough to leave the tap accessible. Perennials and edging plants in front.

**Key considerations:**
- Rain shadow: amend soil deeply, add organic matter, irrigate in first two summers
- Climber must be self-supporting on trellis (no self-clinging species on Victorian brick)
- Tap must remain physically accessible — no planting within 40 cm

---

### Zone 3 — Front Boundary Bed (≈ 0.75 m deep × ≈ 3.5 m long effective ≈ 2.6 sq m)

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Light** | Open to sky to south and west; full afternoon/evening sun; some fence shadow in early morning |
| **Soil** | Clay, reasonably moist — best planting conditions in the garden |
| **Purpose** | PRIMARY display bed — privacy screening, fence softening, main flowering show |
| **Height limit** | Back row 1.5–2.0 m (at fence); front row 0.3–0.6 m |
| **Constraints** | Shed sits immediately behind this bed on the right side |

**Sub-zones:**

| Sub-zone | Location | Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **3a** | Left section (path edge to mid-garden) | ≈ 2.0–2.5 m | Main privacy planting + flowering; full bed depth available |
| **3b** | Right section (in front of shed) | ≈ 1.0–1.5 m | Lower planting; must not block shed doors; bed depth limited by shed proximity |

**Planting approach:**
- **Zone 3a (left):** This is the primary planting area. Back row: 1–2 tall evergreen shrubs (1.5–2.0 m) for privacy. Mid row: 1 flowering shrub. Front row: perennials and edging.
- **Zone 3b (right, in front of shed):** Lower planting only (≤ 0.8 m) to maintain shed door clearance. One compact flowering shrub or perennials only.

**Key considerations:**
- Back-row evergreens provide the privacy screening — select plants that are dense but not hedge-clipped
- This bed gets the best light in the garden and is the main display from the street
- Right end transitions to shed — keep planting low enough that shed doors open freely

---

### Zone 4 — Central Shingle Court (≈ 2.5 m × 3.0 m ≈ 7.5 sq m)

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Light** | Full afternoon sun |
| **Surface** | Retained shingle |
| **Purpose** | Bike circulation, visual openness, garden "breathing space" |
| **Constraints** | 1.0 m clearance around shed doors; bike turning space; gate access |

**Planting approach:** Mostly open shingle. Optional single focal specimen planted through the shingle, positioned centrally or offset to the left to maximise shed access.

**Key considerations:**
- Any planted specimen must allow a clear 1.0 m corridor from the path entry to the shed doors
- A focal plant with a clear stem (standard form or naturally high-branching) works best — keeps ground level clear
- If in doubt, leave unplanted; the perimeter beds will provide ample interest

## 8. Central Focal Plant Options

The central shingle court offers space for a single specimen that provides a visual anchor without obstructing bike circulation. The plant should be positioned centrally or offset slightly left (away from the shed).

### Option 1 — Prunus incisa 'Kojo-no-mai' (Fuji Cherry)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mature height** | 2.0–2.5 m (very slow: ≈ 5 cm/year) |
| **Mature width** | 1.5–2.0 m |
| **Habit** | Upright-spreading with distinctive zigzag branching |
| **Interest: Spring** | Masses of pale pink buds opening white, March–April |
| **Interest: Summer** | Small fresh green leaves |
| **Interest: Autumn** | Orange-red autumn colour |
| **Interest: Winter** | Architectural zigzag bare branches — beautiful silhouette |
| **Maintenance** | Low. Light formative prune after flowering if needed. No regular pruning required |
| **Clay tolerance** | Good |
| **Why suitable** | Four-season interest. Genuinely compact — will not become a large tree even after 20 years. The high branching habit keeps ground level clear for bikes. Very commonly planted in quality London front gardens. The zigzag winter silhouette is a distinctive feature when viewed from the street |

### Option 2 — Magnolia stellata (Star Magnolia)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mature height** | 2.0–3.0 m (slow: takes 10+ years to reach 2 m) |
| **Mature width** | 2.0–2.5 m |
| **Habit** | Rounded, bushy, multi-stemmed |
| **Interest: Spring** | Star-shaped white fragrant flowers on bare branches, March–April |
| **Interest: Summer** | Fresh green foliage |
| **Interest: Autumn** | Yellow autumn colour |
| **Interest: Winter** | Furry grey flower buds visible on bare branches |
| **Maintenance** | Low. No regular pruning — dislikes hard pruning. Mulch root zone annually |
| **Clay tolerance** | Good, prefers slightly acid but tolerates neutral/slightly alkaline clay |
| **Why suitable** | Stunning spring flowering display — one of the finest small garden trees. Genuinely compact. Classic choice for London front gardens. The bushy habit is wider than Prunus incisa, so position carefully to maintain the 1 m bike corridor. West-facing is ideal — avoids early morning sun on frosted buds (a common problem with east-facing magnolias) |

### Option 3 — Amelanchier lamarckii (Snowy Mespilus) — multi-stem specimen

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mature height** | 3.0–4.0 m (moderate growth; can be pruned to 2.5 m) |
| **Mature width** | 2.0–3.0 m |
| **Habit** | Multi-stemmed, open, airy canopy |
| **Interest: Spring** | Bronze-copper new leaves + clouds of white star flowers, April |
| **Interest: Summer** | Small edible dark berries; fresh green leaves |
| **Interest: Autumn** | Spectacular orange-red autumn colour |
| **Interest: Winter** | Elegant bare branch structure |
| **Maintenance** | Low–moderate. Can be selectively thinned to keep open and compact. Will need occasional size management in this space |
| **Clay tolerance** | Good |
| **Why suitable** | Four-season interest and one of the most beautiful small garden trees available. The multi-stem form is airy, casting light shade rather than blocking views. Very popular in contemporary London garden design. **Caution:** this is the largest option — will eventually need management to keep to scale. Best lifted to a clear stem of 1.0–1.2 m to allow bike passage beneath. Only recommended if the client is prepared for occasional size-management pruning every 2–3 years |

### Option 4 — Hydrangea paniculata 'Limelight' (trained as standard)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mature height** | 1.5–2.0 m (on standard stem) |
| **Mature width** | 1.0–1.5 m |
| **Habit** | Lollipop form on clear stem (grafted standard or trained) |
| **Interest: Spring** | Fresh green foliage |
| **Interest: Summer** | Enormous lime-green panicles turning white, Jul–Sep |
| **Interest: Autumn** | Flower heads turn dusty pink; dried heads persist |
| **Interest: Winter** | Architectural stem; dried flower heads if left |
| **Maintenance** | Moderate. Prune back hard to framework in late Feb/early March each year. Feed in spring. Water in dry summers |
| **Clay tolerance** | Good |
| **Why suitable** | Massive summer–autumn flowering display in a very controlled form. The standard stem keeps all foliage above 0.8–1.0 m, leaving ground level completely clear for bikes. Very Bedford Park — hydrangeas are the signature plant of the area. Pairs beautifully with the boundary hydrangeas for a coordinated scheme. The most "local character" option of the four |

## 14. Final Recommended Planting Scheme

### Recommendation: **Scheme C — "Scented Seasons"** (with selective elements from Scheme A)

**Rationale:**

1. **Daily experience is paramount.** This is a front garden — the residents walk through it twice a day. Fragrance transforms a short walk from gate to door into a sensory experience. Scheme C delivers scent in every season.

2. **Winter interest is critical.** The brief explicitly states "winter appearance should not feel empty." Scheme C has the strongest winter display: Sarcococca fragrance, Daphne fragrance, Helleborus flowers, snowdrops, and the Prunus winter silhouette. The evergreen framework from Viburnum, Sarcococca, Clematis armandii, and Ajuga ensures green structure.

3. **The spring peak is spectacular.** Clematis armandii covering the trellis in March–April simultaneously with Prunus blossom creates a genuine "wow" moment visible from the street — highly appropriate for Bedford Park where front gardens are part of the streetscape.

4. **Structured but natural** — the brief's core style direction. Scheme C achieves this: the evergreen shrubs provide structure, while Astrantia, Geranium, and Erigeron provide the naturalistic softness. It's tidier than a cottage garden, softer than a formal scheme.

5. **Privacy is well served.** Viburnum tinus + Sarcococca at the boundary give dense evergreen screening without looking like a clipped hedge. Their natural forms are full and softly rounded.

6. **Prunus incisa 'Kojo-no-mai' is the ideal focal plant for this site.** It is genuinely compact (will not outgrow the space), provides four-season interest, requires minimal maintenance, and its high branching habit keeps ground level clear for bikes.

### Planting Notes

1. **Soil preparation:** Dig in generous organic matter (garden compost or well-rotted manure) to all beds. London clay benefits enormously from structure improvement. Add grit to Zone 2 (house wall rain shadow).

2. **Planting time:** Shrubs and perennials: October–March (avoid frozen ground). Clematis: spring. Bulbs: Galanthus "in the green" Feb–Mar; Cyclamen tubers Aug–Sep.

3. **First-year watering:** All plants will need regular watering in the first summer, especially Zone 2 (rain shadow) and Zone 4 focal plant. Drip irrigation or a watering routine using the existing tap.

4. **Mulch:** Apply 5–8 cm bark mulch to all beds after planting. Replenish annually. Keeps clay moist in summer, suppresses weeds.

5. **Annual maintenance calendar:**

| Month | Task |
|---|---|
| Feb–Mar | Cut back Hydrangea 'Annabelle' to 30 cm; tidy Epimedium/Helleborus old leaves; plant snowdrops in the green |
| Mar–Apr | Light trim of Clematis armandii after flowering (remove dead/wayward); feed all plants |
| May–Jun | Chelsea chop Geranium if leggy; deadhead Erigeron |
| Jul–Aug | Water in dry spells; deadhead Astrantia for repeat |
| Sep–Oct | Plant spring bulbs; cut back spent perennials; mulch |
| Nov–Dec | Light prune Sarcococca if needed; enjoy fragrance! |
