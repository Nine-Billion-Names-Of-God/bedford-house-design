# Flower Bed Planting Design — London Terraced Garden

---

## 1. Summary of Constraints

### Site geometry

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Garden width | 6.2 m |
| Patio depth | 3.1 m |
| Bed length | 2.1 m (starting 0.5 m from house wall, ending 0.5 m before patio edge) |
| Left bed width | 0.2–0.3 m (tapered) |
| Right bed width | 0.3 m |
| Planting depth | 0.2–0.3 m (very shallow) |

### Environmental conditions

| Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| Orientation | East-facing |
| Morning | Direct sun |
| Afternoon | Shade |
| Soil | London clay |
| Boundaries | Wooden slatted fences both sides |

### Functional constraints

- **Water tap** on right side near house — must remain accessible (no planting within ~20 cm of tap)
- **Right bed** = primary herb zone (nearest kitchen door)
- **Left bed** = secondary planting (herbs + flowers)
- Plants must not obstruct patio or spill onto walking surfaces
- Low to moderate maintenance required

### Critical planting constraints

- Beds are **extremely narrow** (20–30 cm) — single-row planting only
- Soil is **very shallow** (20–30 cm) — only shallow-rooting, compact plants
- No large shrubs or trees
- No deep-rooted plants
- Climbers must be non-invasive and manageable

## 2. Overall Planting Strategy

### Understanding the spatial challenge

These beds are exceptionally narrow. At 20–30 cm wide, we are working with essentially a **single line of plants** with no room for layered depth planting. This means:

- All plants must be arranged in a **linear sequence** along the 2.1 m length
- Vertical interest comes from **climbers on the fence** and **upright herbs**
- Ground-level interest comes from **low edging plants** at the patio-facing edge
- The repeating pattern runs **along the length**, not across the width

### Planting structure (front to back within the 20–30 cm width)

Since the beds are only 20–30 cm wide, the realistic approach is:

1. **Fence line (back):** 1 × climber planted at the midpoint of each bed, trained along the fence
2. **Main line (centre):** Repeating pattern of herbs and flowering plants along the 2.1 m length
3. **Patio edge (front):** Low creeping herbs or edging plants to soften the transition

In the narrowest sections of the left bed (20 cm), only a single row is possible. In the right bed (30 cm), a very tight two-row arrangement is feasible — climber at the back, herbs/flowers at the front.

### Plant count per bed

Each bed is 2.1 m long. With compact plants spaced at 15–25 cm centres:

- **8–12 plants per bed** (main line, excluding climber)
- **1 climber per bed** (planted at or near centre)
- **2–4 edging plants per bed** (at patio-facing front edge)

### Planting ratio target

| Layer | Proportion | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Herbs | 60–70% | Primary — culinary use, fragrance |
| Flowering plants | 20–30% | Supporting — colour, pollinator interest |
| Climbers | 1 per bed | Vertical — soften fence, green backdrop |
| Edging | 2–4 per bed | Ground — soften patio edge |

### Repetition approach

Rather than a random cottage-garden mix, planting follows simple **A–B–A–B** or **A–B–C–A–B–C** repeating patterns along the length of each bed. This creates visual rhythm in a very small space and makes maintenance straightforward.

### Soil improvement note

London clay in 20–30 cm depth will benefit from:
- Mixing in horticultural grit and organic compost before planting
- Adding a thin layer of gravel mulch (2–3 cm) to improve drainage around herb crowns
- This is especially important for Mediterranean herbs (thyme, rosemary, sage) which dislike waterlogged clay

### Scheme A — Maintenance Notes

- **Rosemary:** Prune lightly after flowering (late spring). Do not cut into old wood. Upright variety keeps it narrow.
- **Thyme:** Shear back after flowering in midsummer. Replace every 3–4 years as plants become woody.
- **Sage:** Prune in spring; pinch growing tips to keep compact. Replace every 4–5 years.
- **Lavender:** Trim after flowering; never cut below green growth.
- **Chives:** Cut to ground level several times per season to encourage fresh growth.
- **Star Jasmine:** Tie in new growth to horizontal wires on fence. Prune after flowering to contain.
- **Clematis 'Nelly Moser':** Pruning Group 2 — light prune in late winter, removing dead/weak stems only.
- **Creeping Thyme:** Minimal care. Trim edges if it spreads beyond the bed.

### Scheme B — Maintenance Notes

- **Erigeron:** Self-seeds gently. Trim back hard in early spring to refresh. Extremely long flowering season (May–October).
- **Hardy Geranium 'Rozanne':** Cut back midsummer if leggy — will reflush with flowers. One of the longest-flowering perennials.
- **Roman Chamomile:** Mow or trim lightly to keep dense. Non-flowering variety ('Treneague') also available if purely for edging.
- **Parsley:** Biennial — replace annually or every two years. Sow new plants in spring.
- **Climbing Hydrangea:** Slow to establish (2–3 years). Once settled, prune after flowering to maintain shape. Self-clinging — needs no wires, though initial support helps. Excellent for east-facing shade.

### Scheme C — Maintenance Notes

- **Heuchera:** Evergreen. Lift and replant every 3 years as crown lifts from soil. Remove spent flower spikes.
- **Salvia 'Hot Lips':** Cut back hard in spring (to 15 cm). Flowers continuously June–October. May die back in harsh winters — mulch crown.
- **Ajuga:** Spreads by runners — trim edges annually to keep within bed.
- **Golden Oregano:** Slightly less vigorous than green form. Brighter in part-shade. Trim after flowering.
- **Pratia:** Tolerates light foot traffic. Spreads gently — trim if it crosses onto paving.
- **Clematis 'Apple Blossom':** Evergreen — pruning Group 1 (prune only to control size, after flowering in spring). Needs wires or trellis.

## 7. Climbers Summary

Climbers are mandatory in all schemes. One climber per bed, planted at or near the midpoint of the bed against the fence.

### Climber options across schemes

| Climber | Used In | Type | Evergreen | Height (trained) | Flowers | Support Required | East-Facing Suitable | Clay Tolerant |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Star Jasmine (*Trachelospermum jasminoides*) | A, B, C (right bed) | Twining | Yes | 2–3 m | White, scented, summer | Horizontal wires or trellis | Yes (sheltered) | Yes |
| Clematis 'Nelly Moser' | A (left bed) | Tendril | No (deciduous) | 2–3 m | Large pink-mauve, late spring | Trellis or wire mesh | Excellent (prefers not full south sun) | Yes |
| Climbing Hydrangea (*H. anomala petiolaris*) | B (left bed) | Self-clinging | No (deciduous) | 2–3 m | White lace-cap, summer | Minimal (self-clinging) | Excellent (shade-tolerant) | Yes |
| Clematis 'Apple Blossom' (*C. armandii*) | C (left bed) | Twining | Yes | 2–3 m | Pink-white, scented, early spring | Wires or trellis | Yes (sheltered) | Yes |

### Support requirements

| Support Type | Detail | Applicable Climbers |
|---|---|---|
| **Horizontal wires** | Vine eyes screwed into fence posts at 30–40 cm intervals vertically. Galvanised wire stretched between. Cheapest and least visible option. | Star Jasmine, Clematis 'Apple Blossom' |
| **Trellis panel** | Thin timber or willow trellis fixed 2–5 cm off the fence face (to allow air circulation and twining). | Clematis 'Nelly Moser', Star Jasmine |
| **None / minimal** | Self-clinging via aerial roots. May need initial ties for first year until established. | Climbing Hydrangea |

### Climber planting notes for shallow beds

- Climbers are the most demanding plants in these beds regarding root depth
- Plant the climber at the **deepest point** of the bed (against the fence where soil is deepest)
- Dig as deeply as possible (minimum 25 cm) and enrich the planting hole with compost
- Water regularly during the first two growing seasons
- For Star Jasmine: provide winter mulch in the first 2–3 years (slightly tender when young)
- For Clematis: plant the crown 5–8 cm deeper than the pot level to encourage basal shoots

## 9. Final Recommended Scheme

### Recommendation: **Scheme B — Balanced Herb + Flowers**

**Rationale:**

1. **Best fit for east-facing aspect.** The Climbing Hydrangea (left bed) is one of the finest climbers for shade and part-shade — it will thrive on this aspect better than more sun-hungry alternatives. Geranium 'Rozanne' is also outstanding in part-shade.

2. **Longest season of interest.** Mexican Fleabane flowers for 5+ months. Geranium 'Rozanne' flowers for 4+ months. Combined with evergreen rosemary and Star Jasmine, there is something of interest in every month of the year.

3. **Practical herb core maintained.** Rosemary, thyme, sage, lavender, chives, and parsley cover the most commonly used kitchen herbs. All are positioned for easy harvesting from the patio.

4. **Forgiving on clay soil.** Geranium, Hydrangea, and chamomile are all naturally tolerant of heavier soils. The herbs benefit from the grit/compost soil improvement recommended above.

5. **Low-moderate maintenance.** Only parsley needs regular replacement. All other plants are long-lived perennials.

6. **Chamomile edging is a bonus.** Roman chamomile releases fragrance when touched — pleasant along a patio edge where hands or feet may brush it.

### If you prefer maximum herb use: choose Scheme A.
### If you prefer maximum visual impact: choose Scheme C.

## Appendix: Soil Preparation for Shallow Clay Beds

Before planting any scheme, prepare the beds as follows:

1. **Excavate** existing soil to the full available depth (20–30 cm)
2. **Mix in** equal parts:
   - Horticultural grit (sharp sand) — improves drainage
   - Multi-purpose compost or well-rotted garden compost — improves structure and nutrients
3. **Ratio:** approximately 1 part existing clay : 1 part grit : 1 part compost
4. **Backfill** beds with the improved mix
5. **Top-dress** with 2–3 cm fine gravel mulch (especially around Mediterranean herbs)
6. **Allow** the beds to settle for 1–2 weeks before planting if possible

This preparation is essential for herb survival in shallow London clay. Without it, waterlogging will kill Mediterranean herbs within a year.
