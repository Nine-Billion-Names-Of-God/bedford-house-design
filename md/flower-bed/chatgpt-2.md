## 1. Summary of constraints

I understand the planting area as **two very narrow side beds flanking the patio**, each about **2.1 m long**, with the **right bed 0.3 m wide** and the **left bed tapering from about 0.2–0.3 m**. The garden is **east-facing**, so the beds get **morning sun and afternoon shade**. Soil is **London clay**, planting depth is only **20–30 cm**, and the **right-hand bed must stay easy to use as a kitchen herb strip without blocking the water tap**.

That means the planting needs to be:

| Constraint               | What it means for planting                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Very narrow beds         | No large shrubs, no bulky mounds, no wide-sprawling plants                                                                        |
| Shallow depth (20–30 cm) | Use compact, shallow-rooting, manageable plants                                                                                   |
| East-facing              | Plants should cope with sun early in the day, then shade later                                                                    |
| London clay              | Best with tough, widely available garden-centre plants; avoid plants that demand very sharp alpine drainage unless used sparingly |
| Right bed near tap       | Keep the first section low and simple; no thorny, woody, or bulky plants there                                                    |
| Herb-forward brief       | Most planting should be culinary herbs, with flowers as accents                                                                   |
| Fence edges              | Climbers are important to add height without taking floor space                                                                   |

## 2. Overall planting strategy

Because the beds are so narrow, these should be treated less like traditional borders and more like **linear planted ribbons**.

### Recommended structure

| Layer               | How it works here                                                                       |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Vertical layer      | **One compact climber per bed** trained flat against the fence                          |
| Main planting layer | Repeating **compact herbs in a single-file or lightly staggered rhythm**                |
| Accent layer        | Small pockets of flowering plants to soften and brighten                                |
| Edge layer          | Low edging herbs or neat groundcover to soften the paving edge without spilling too far |

### Best approach for these beds

- Use **repetition**, not mixed “collector planting”.
- Keep the **right bed lower and more practical**.
- Put the **strongest flowering mix in the left bed**, where it can be a little softer.
- Use only **compact structural plants** such as dwarf lavender, compact sage, or heuchera.
- Keep the **first 45–60 cm of the right bed nearest the house/tap especially low**, mainly thyme, chives, parsley-type planting.
- Use **one climber per bed only**, trained tightly to fence wires or a slim trellis.

A good mental model is:

**fence climber + repeating herb mounds + low edging accents**

# 3. Planting Scheme A — herb-forward

**Character:** most practical and kitchen-friendly
**Look:** silver-green, mauve, white, fresh and restrained
**Best for:** strongest herb use, easiest long-term control

### Suggested planting rhythm

- **Right bed:** thyme → chives → oregano → thyme → parsley/chives
- **Left bed:** dwarf lavender → thyme → marjoram → lavender → thyme, with small flowering pockets between

### Scheme A — Right bed (primary herb zone)

| Plant name                                                        | Qty | Spacing          | Typical mature H × S    | Role          |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------- | --: | ---------------- | ----------------------- | ------------- |
| Lemon thyme (_Thymus x citriodorus_)                              |   2 | 30 cm centres    | 15–20 cm × 25–30 cm     | Herb          |
| Creeping thyme (_Thymus serpyllum_)                               |   3 | 25 cm centres    | 5–10 cm × 25–30 cm      | Herb / edging |
| Chives (_Allium schoenoprasum_)                                   |   2 | 25–30 cm centres | 25–30 cm × 20 cm        | Herb          |
| Compact oregano (_Origanum vulgare_ ‘Compactum’)                  |   2 | 30 cm centres    | 20–30 cm × 25–30 cm     | Herb          |
| Flat-leaf parsley                                                 |   1 | 25 cm centre     | 25–30 cm × 25 cm        | Herb          |
| Clematis alpina or macropetala type (for example ‘Frances Rivis’) |   1 | 1 plant in bed   | 180–250 cm trained flat | Climber       |

**Maintenance notes**

- Keep the **parsley and thyme nearest the tap**.
- Trim oregano and thyme lightly after flowering.
- Divide chives every 2–3 years.
- Replace parsley as needed because it is short-lived/biennial.
- Keep clematis tied flat and away from the tap zone.

### Scheme A — Left bed (herbs + flowers)

| Plant name                                                         | Qty | Spacing          | Typical mature H × S    | Role                     |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------ | --: | ---------------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Dwarf lavender (_Lavandula angustifolia_ ‘Little Lady’ or similar) |   2 | 35–40 cm centres | 30–40 cm × 30–40 cm     | Herb / compact structure |
| Lemon thyme                                                        |   2 | 30 cm centres    | 15–20 cm × 25–30 cm     | Herb                     |
| Sweet marjoram                                                     |   2 | 30 cm centres    | 20–30 cm × 25–30 cm     | Herb                     |
| Chives                                                             |   1 | 25–30 cm centre  | 25–30 cm × 20 cm        | Herb                     |
| Erigeron karvinskianus                                             |   2 | 30 cm centres    | 25–35 cm × 30 cm        | Flowering                |
| Armeria maritima                                                   |   1 | 25 cm centre     | 15–20 cm × 20 cm        | Flowering / edging       |
| Clematis alpina type                                               |   1 | 1 plant in bed   | 180–250 cm trained flat | Climber                  |

**Maintenance notes**

- Trim lavender lightly after flowering; do not cut into old wood.
- Erigeron should be clipped back once or twice a year so it does not flop over the paving.
- Marjoram can be cut regularly for kitchen use and to keep it tight.

# 6. Climbers summary (across schemes)

Because the beds are so shallow and narrow, the climbers should be treated as a **flat vertical screen**, not as free-standing ramblers.

| Scheme | Recommended climber                   | Why it works                                                      | Support needed                                                 |
| ------ | ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| A      | Clematis alpina / macropetala type    | Light, elegant, not too heavy-looking, good for east-facing fence | Slim trellis or taut horizontal wires with thin vertical canes |
| B      | Clematis ‘Piilu’ or ‘Josephine’       | Compact, more floral impact, manageable size                      | Slim trellis or wire grid fixed tightly to fence               |
| C      | Clematis ‘Nelly Moser’ or ‘Josephine’ | Strong ornamental value, good in morning sun / afternoon shade    | Same as above; keep trained flat and pruned neatly             |

### Support requirements

- Use **one climber per bed** only.
- Best support is **thin wires or a narrow trellis panel**, because clematis climb by leaf stalks and need slim supports.
- Fix supports **flat to the fence**, not projecting out into the bed.
- On the **right bed**, keep the climber **at least 45 cm away from the tap zone**.
- Tie in young stems early so they do not lean into the patio.

# 8. Final recommended planting scheme

## Recommended: **Scheme A — herb-forward**

This is the strongest fit for your brief because it best respects the real-world constraints:

- the beds are **extremely narrow**
- the soil is **shallow and clay-based**
- the **right bed must remain genuinely usable**
- the brief prioritises **herbs first**
- the design needs to stay **practical, tidy, and not spill onto the patio**

Scheme A gives you the clearest and most durable outcome:

- **right side = genuinely useful herb strip**
- **left side = softened herb-and-flower version of the same language**
- **climbers provide the height**, so the ground planting can stay small and manageable

My suggested final direction would be:

- **Right bed:** thyme, chives, oregano, parsley, one compact clematis
- **Left bed:** dwarf lavender, thyme, marjoram, chives, erigeron/armeria, one compact clematis

That will feel coherent, fresh, green, and practical without forcing oversized plants into undersized beds.
