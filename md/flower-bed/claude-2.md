# Planting Design — Narrow Flower Beds

## Back Garden, Victorian Terrace, London

---

## 1. Summary of Constraints

| Constraint | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Garden width** | 6.2 m |
| **Patio depth** | 3.1 m |
| **Bed length** | 2.1 m each (0.5 m offset from house wall; 0.5 m before patio edge) |
| **Right bed width** | 0.3 m (uniform) |
| **Left bed width** | 0.2–0.3 m (tapered) |
| **Planting depth** | 0.2–0.3 m (very shallow) |
| **Orientation** | East-facing — morning sun, afternoon shade |
| **Soil** | London clay |
| **Boundaries** | Wooden slatted fences both sides |
| **Water tap** | Right side, near house wall — must remain accessible |
| **Right bed function** | Primary herb zone (kitchen proximity) |
| **Left bed function** | Herbs + flowering plants |
| **Planting ratio** | 60–70 % herbs · 30–40 % flowering/ornamental |
| **Climbers** | Mandatory on both fences — non-invasive, manageable |
| **Planting style** | Repetition-based patterns, not random mixes |
| **Avoid** | Large shrubs, deep-rooted plants, patio obstruction |

### Critical Implications

- Beds are essentially **single-row linear strips** (20–30 cm is too narrow for double-row planting).
- Soil depth of 20–30 cm restricts choices to **shallow-rooting, compact plants**.
- East-facing aspect means plants must tolerate **part shade** (only ~4–5 hours of direct morning sun in summer).
- London clay retains moisture — avoid plants requiring sharp drainage (most Mediterranean herbs cope if clay is amended with grit).
- The right bed's first 30–40 cm (nearest the house) should be planted **low or left clear** to maintain water-tap access.

## 2. Overall Planting Strategy

### Structure

Each 2.1 m bed is treated as a **linear planting strip** with three functional layers:

| Layer | Role | Position |
|---|---|---|
| **Vertical (climber)** | Greens the fence; adds height and softness | One climber per bed, planted centrally or at the far end from the house |
| **Mid layer (herbs + flowers)** | Core planting; upright herbs 20–40 cm tall | Repeating pattern along the bed length |
| **Edge / ground layer** | Softens the patio edge; keeps planting tidy | Low, mat-forming or clumping plants at the patio-facing edge |

### Planting Pattern Logic

With ~2.1 m of length and plants spaced at 20–25 cm, each bed holds approximately **8–10 plants** (excluding the climber). A simple **A → B → A → B** or **A → B → C → A → B → C** repeating module works best.

### Soil Preparation (All Schemes)

- Dig out existing clay to full depth (20–30 cm).
- Mix in **horticultural grit** (30 % by volume) and **peat-free compost** (30 %) to improve drainage and root penetration.
- For the climber planting pocket, excavate a slightly wider and deeper hole (35–40 cm if possible) and backfill with the same improved mix.
- Top-dress annually with a thin layer of compost.

## 7. Climbers — Detailed Notes

### Support Requirements

All climbers in this design need **support fixed to the fence**. Two options:

1. **Horizontal wires:** Galvanised wire at 30 cm intervals, fixed to vine eyes screwed into fence posts. Best for clematis (twining leaf stalks grip wires).
2. **Narrow trellis panel:** A 30–40 cm-wide trellis attached to the fence. Best for jasmine and honeysuckle (ties needed).

### Planting in Shallow Soil

- Excavate the climber planting pocket to **35–40 cm deep** if possible (even slightly below the general bed depth).
- Backfill with grit-enriched compost.
- Clematis: Plant the crown **5–10 cm deeper** than the pot level (protects against clematis wilt).
- Mulch around the base with gravel or bark to keep roots cool.
- Water regularly in the first year to establish roots.

### Climber Comparison

| Climber | Best For | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Clematis alpina** | Both beds | Compact; flowers in shade; low maintenance; non-invasive | Deciduous; flowers early (April–May) |
| **Clematis 'Arabella'** | Right bed | Long flowering (June–Sept); herbaceous so dies back neatly | Needs regular tying; not self-supporting |
| **Winter Jasmine** | Left bed | Flowers in winter; very tough; tolerates deep shade | Can look untidy; needs tying in |
| **Honeysuckle 'Serotina'** | Left bed | Scented; attracts wildlife; native | Moderate vigour — needs annual pruning |
| **Star Jasmine** | Left bed (sheltered) | Evergreen; scented; elegant | Needs warmth; struggles in exposed/cold spots; wants improved drainage |

## 8. Maintenance Notes

### Seasonal Tasks

| Season | Task |
|---|---|
| **Spring (March–April)** | Cut back dead growth on herbaceous plants. Trim thyme lightly. Top-dress beds with compost. Tie in climber new growth. |
| **Summer (May–Aug)** | Harvest herbs regularly (this is the best pruning). Deadhead erigeron and catmint after first flush. Water climbers in dry spells. |
| **Autumn (Sept–Oct)** | Cut back catmint/nepeta. Leave chives to die back naturally. Plant replacement parsley (biennial). |
| **Winter (Nov–Feb)** | Prune winter jasmine after flowering. Clematis alpina: no routine pruning needed. Check fence wires/ties. |

### Key Maintenance Points

- **Thyme:** Trim after flowering (never cut into old wood — it won't regenerate).
- **Sage:** Pinch tips regularly to prevent it exceeding 25 cm spread; replace every 4–5 years.
- **Parsley:** Biennial — sow/plant fresh each year for continuous supply.
- **Chives:** Cut to ground level after flowering to encourage fresh growth.
- **Erigeron:** May self-seed into patio cracks — pull unwanted seedlings.
- **Honeysuckle:** Prune after flowering to keep within bounds; can be hard-pruned if needed.
- **Clematis alpina:** Rarely needs pruning — just remove dead/damaged stems.

## 10. Final Recommendation

### Recommended Scheme: **Scheme B — Balanced Herb + Flowers**

**Rationale:**

1. **Practical:** Retains a herb-dominant right bed (100 % herbs) near the kitchen.
2. **Beautiful:** The left bed's catmint and honeysuckle provide colour, scent, and pollinator value from June through September.
3. **Reliable:** All plants are widely available, clay-tolerant, and proven in London gardens.
4. **Year-round interest:** Evergreen thyme in winter; honeysuckle and clematis in summer; chive flowers in early summer.
5. **Manageable:** Moderate maintenance with no high-risk plants.
6. **Fragrant:** Both honeysuckle (evening scent on the patio) and lemon thyme provide sensory pleasure.

**Key adjustment for the left bed's taper:** Plant catmint at the wider (0.3 m) end and creeping thyme at the narrow (0.2 m) end. This respects the tapered geometry naturally.

### Suggested Modification

If the honeysuckle proves too vigorous for the narrow left bed, substitute with **Clematis alpina 'Frances Rivis'** (from Scheme A) — this is the safest climber for constrained spaces and requires almost no pruning.
