## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

### Wall Roles

| Wall | Role | Depth | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Back wall (298 cm wide) | Primary storage spine — boxes, heavy tools, ladder | 40 cm | Full-height 5-level shelving; 2 m ladder on horizontal brackets at 168 cm |
| Right side wall (175 cm deep) | Workbench bay + tool board + front open zone | 60 cm (rear 120 cm) | Workbench surface at 90 cm; tiles + Kärcher under bench; dedicated tool board 90–165 cm on wall face |
| Left side wall (175 cm deep) | Secondary shelving + long-handle garden tool zone | 40 cm (rear 120 cm) | Front 55 cm: long-handle garden tools on wall hooks |

### Why mixed depth is the right approach

The tile format (60 × 120 cm) is the defining constraint. Tiles stored flat need exactly 60 cm of bay depth. Placing this 60 cm zone on the right side wall — integrated with the workbench — achieves three things at once: (1) tiles sit naturally in a bay that is exactly the right depth, (2) the workbench itself benefits from 55–65 cm of comfortable reach depth, and (3) bulky occasional items (Kärcher, toolbox) share the under-bench bay without wasted space. Keeping all other storage at 40 cm depth maximises the central floor width and avoids the shed feeling claustrophobic.

### How central floor space is preserved

Back wall shelves: 40 cm deep, leaving 135 cm of clear depth from back to shelf face. Left wall rear shelving: 40 cm deep. Right wall workbench zone: 60 cm deep but restricted to the rear 120 cm of the right wall only — the front 55 cm of the right wall is completely open at floor level. The unobstructed central floor area is approximately **198 cm wide × 120 cm deep** (~2.4 m²). Both doors open outward so entry is never blocked.

### Workbench rationale

A 90 cm high workbench is the standard ergonomic height for standing tool work. Positioned on the right wall rear section (120 cm long × 60 cm deep), it creates a coherent workstation hub:

- **Tool board** on the right wall face immediately above the bench (90–165 cm) puts all hand tools and cutters within arm's reach while standing at the bench
- **Under-bench bay** (0–88 cm) holds tiles, Kärcher, and toolbox — all infrequent or occasional items that sit naturally at floor level
- **Back-right corner shelves** (30 cm deep, mounted on the back wall face above bench height) provide secondary storage for accessories, oils, and extension cord visible from the workbench

### Ladder strategy

- **2 m non-foldable ladder**: horizontal wall-mounted L-brackets on the back wall at 168 cm height, spanning X=20–215 cm. Stored above head height; easily unhooked with two hands.
- **1.5 m foldable ladder**: stored vertically in the right front open zone (Y=10–50 cm from front wall) on two strap/bracket hooks on the right wall face at Z=60 cm and Z=140 cm.

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A — Back Wall Dominant

**Description:** All primary storage concentrated on the back wall as full-width, 5–6 level, floor-to-eave shelving. Side walls left largely open or with minimal rear shelving only. No workbench. Tiles leaning in a corner. Tools hung loosely on one side wall.

**Strengths:**
- Maximum shelf running metres on a single accessible face
- Simplest single-structure build
- Maximum central floor space (back wall at 40 cm is the only intrusion)
- No complex joinery

**Weaknesses:**
- No 60 cm deep zone anywhere — tiles have no proper home; leaning is a trip and breakage hazard
- No workbench — no comfortable surface for DIY tasks
- Tool organisation unstructured — hand tools, garden tools, and boxes compete for back wall hooks and shelves
- BBQ and ladders claim central floor space permanently
- Under-utilises the two side walls; total storage capacity is lower than the brief demands

---

### Concept B — Right Side Bay (Workbench + 60 cm) + Back Wall Spine + Left Secondary *(selected)*

**Description:** Right side wall rear 120 cm becomes the dedicated 60 cm deep workbench bay. Under bench: tiles flat on floor, Kärcher, toolbox. Tool board on right wall face (90–165 cm). Back wall: 238 cm wide, 40 cm deep, 5-level shelving. Left side wall rear 120 cm: 40 cm deep, 5-level shelving. Right front open zone: foldable ladder (vertical) + BBQ (floor). Left front zone: long-handle garden tool hooks.

**Strengths:**
- 60 cm zone satisfies tile + Kärcher + toolbox storage in one coherent bay
- Tool board directly in front of person standing at bench — best possible ergonomics for frequently used hand tools
- Back wall remains 40 cm deep throughout — preserves maximum central floor width
- Three clear and spatially separated functional zones: work, box/general storage, garden tools
- BBQ and foldable ladder contained in a dedicated zone near the door without obstructing entry

**Weaknesses:**
- Workbench reduces total right-wall shelf capacity (120 cm of right wall is workbench, not open shelving)
- Under-bench access for tiles and Kärcher requires bending — acceptable given these are infrequent items

---

### Concept C — Back Wall Workbench + Side Shelving

**Description:** Workbench at the back wall right section (~120 cm wide), projecting 60 cm into the shed. Back wall left section: 40 cm deep shelving. Both side walls: rear section shelving at 40 cm. Tool board on right side wall face adjacent to back-wall workbench.

**Strengths:**
- Workbench integrated with the widest, most rigid wall
- Right side wall can have a continuous 40 cm deep shelf run for its full rear 120 cm

**Weaknesses:**
- Back wall has a 60 cm deep projection → central floor is 20 cm narrower at that corner (floor clearance ~178 cm vs 198 cm for Concept B)
- Reaching the back-wall shelves on the left section requires leaning over a 60 cm surface — ergonomically inferior for box access
- The tool board (on right side wall) is 90° from the workbench — you must turn away from the bench to reach tools
- The 40 cm / 60 cm depth transition on the same wall is harder to frame and finish cleanly
- Visual and practical imbalance: the shed feels narrower on entry

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Selected: Concept B — Right Side Bay Workbench + Back Wall Spine + Left Secondary**

This design is the strongest solution for the following reasons:

1. **Tile constraint resolved exactly.** The 60 cm deep workbench bay accommodates tiles lying flat on the floor — the bay depth equals the tile width precisely.
2. **Ergonomically coherent workstation.** The tool board is directly in front of the user at the bench; all frequently used hand tools are visible and reachable without turning.
3. **Back wall at uniform 40 cm depth.** This gives the maximum usable central floor width compatible with meaningful storage on all three walls.
4. **Three spatially distinct zones** emerge naturally — work/tools (right rear), box/general storage (back + left rear), garden tools (left front) — making the shed intuitive to use.
5. **BBQ and ladders are parked, not dumped.** The right front open zone is a natural reception area adjacent to the doors, large enough for the BBQ and foldable ladder without blocking the central aisle.
6. **Long-handle garden tools at the door.** The left front wall face has hooks at head height — the first things you reach when entering for garden work.

## 13. Construction Approach

### Recommended approach: Hybrid (shed-wall-leaned + freestanding where critical)

| Element | Approach | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Back wall shelving unit | Freestanding timber frame (ladder frame), top rail screwed to back wall horizontal top plate | Self-supporting; leaning into back wall for lateral stability; no load transferred through thin cladding |
| Left wall shelving unit | Same as back wall — freestanding ladder frame | Identical logic |
| Workbench | Freestanding on 4 legs (44×94 mm), no reliance on right wall for vertical load | Bench loads are high; must not rely on cladding |
| Tool board | Screwed directly into right wall vertical studs (locate studs with detector) | Tool board loads are light and distributed; right wall studs are adequate if correctly located |
| Back-right corner shelves | Cantilevered from back wall studs and right wall studs via L-bracket ledger rails | Light loads; wall-mounted on studs is appropriate |
| Left front tool hooks | Heavy-duty screw hooks into left wall studs | Locate studs; use M6 expanding wall anchors if needed |

### Freestanding frame geometry (back wall and left wall units)

Each shelving unit is a **ladder frame**: two vertical upright assemblies (paired 44×44 mm PAR legs, spaced ~38–40 cm apart to form a 40 cm deep frame) connected by horizontal shelf bearers (44×44 mm) at each shelf height. A top horizontal rail connects the two uprights at eave height and is fixed to the wall top plate with two 10 mm coach bolts. The frame sits on feet; feet are not fixed to the floor (allows easy removal if needed) but a short cleat screwed to the floor prevents forward sliding.

The back wall unit is 238 cm wide and requires 3 intermediate uprights to keep shelf spans ≤80 cm (preventing noticeable shelf sag under box loads). Uprights at X=0, X=79, X=159, X=238 (four uprights = three bays at ~79 cm each).

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. **Clear and prepare the shed floor.** Remove all existing items. Mark floor positions for all frames (chalk line or masking tape).

2. **Locate all wall studs.** Use a stud detector on the back wall, left wall, and right wall. Mark stud positions at 120 cm from floor (visible reference height). Right wall studs are needed for tool board; back wall top plate needed for frame securing.

3. **Build the back wall shelving frame.** Cut all 44×44 mm uprights (8 pieces at 190 cm). Cut all shelf bearers (40 pieces at 40 cm). Assemble on the floor as flat ladder sub-frames, one per bay (3 bays), using 70 mm screws and wood glue at each joint. Stand frames upright and connect with horizontal top and bottom rails. Check plumb. Screw top rail to back wall plate with 100 mm coach screws.

4. **Cut and fit back wall shelf boards (OSB/3 18 mm).** Five shelves per run, each 238 cm × 40 cm. Cut to length, lower into position, screw to bearers from above (50 mm screws, 4 per shelf per bay). Add front lip strips if desired.

5. **Build the left wall shelving frame.** Same ladder-frame technique as step 3. Two uprights, two bays (each ~60 cm span within the 120 cm wide zone). Secure top rail to left wall plate.

6. **Cut and fit left wall shelf boards.** Five shelves, each 120 cm × 40 cm.

7. **Build the workbench.** Cut four legs (44×94 mm, 88 cm), front and back apron rails (120 cm), side rails (60 cm), bottom stretcher. Assemble as a rigid box frame with corner halving joints or simple butt joints + coach bolts. Check square, add diagonal brace on underside if needed. Cut bench top (18 mm birch ply, 120 × 60 cm). Screw bench top to frame from below. Position bench against right wall.

8. **Install tool board.** Cut 18 mm birch ply to 120 × 75 cm. Drill pilot holes. Screw to right wall studs from front with 80 mm screws (minimum 2 per stud). Install peg rails: drill 25 mm holes, glue in dowels. Install wire hooks.

9. **Install back-right corner shelves.** Fix horizontal ledger rails (19×44 mm) to back wall studs at C1/C2/C3 heights. Fix side ledger rails to right wall studs. Drop in 12 mm ply shelf boards (60 × 30 cm each). Screw from above.

10. **Install over-bench shelf.** Cut OSB or ply (120 × 30 cm). Fix ledger rails to right wall studs at 165 cm. Drop in shelf.

11. **Install 2 m ladder brackets.** Screw two heavy-duty L-brackets to back wall at Z=168 cm, X=20 cm and X=215 cm. Test load-bearing before hanging ladder.

12. **Install left front wall hooks.** Locate left wall studs in the front open zone. Fit 5 heavy-duty screw hooks at Z=145–175 cm for long-handle garden tools.

13. **Install foldable ladder strap brackets.** Right wall face, front open zone: two strap/hook brackets at Z=60 cm and Z=140 cm, Y=15–50 cm from front wall.

14. **Apply finishes.** Sand all cut edges. Prime all exposed timber. Apply one coat exterior paint or shed preservative to all surfaces.

15. **Load and organise.** Starting from heavy floor items, work up. Load tiles first (under bench, back of bay), then Kärcher, then toolbox. Load shelves from bottom up. Hang tools on board. Hang ladder. Place BBQ in open zone.

## 19. Final Recommendation

**Concept B — Right Side Bay Workbench + Back Wall Spine + Left Secondary** is the definitive design for this shed.

### Why this design is the best solution

The design resolves every constraint simultaneously without compromise:

- The 60 cm tile bay is built into the workbench zone — not an afterthought.
- The workbench creates a proper working hub: tool board in front, accessories at arm's reach, heavy bench items below.
- The back wall carries the majority of box storage at 40 cm depth, the most efficient and accessible arrangement.
- The left wall carries secondary box storage and — critically — the fertiliser and smaller items at waist height (LS3 at 72 cm) where they are genuinely easy to access frequently.
- The long-handle garden tools are on the left front wall face, the natural first-reach zone when entering through the double doors for garden work.
- The central floor (~2.4 m²) is a genuine working area, not a residual gap between furniture.

### Caveats

- **Stud locations:** The tool board and corner shelves must be fixed to actual studs in the right and back walls. Use a stud detector before marking positions; do not rely on cladding screws as stud indicators.
- **Tile quantity:** The tile bay under the bench accommodates standard tile deliveries. If tiles exceed ~15 units (225 kg), a timber support frame should be added under the tiles rather than relying on bare floor alone.
- **Workbench height:** 90 cm is correct for average adult standing work (~5'8"–6'0"). If the primary user is shorter than 5'6", reduce to 85 cm.
- **BBQ:** If the BBQ is a large gas BBQ with a wide footprint (>65 cm), it may need to move slightly into the central zone. Measure the actual BBQ before finalising the front open zone.
- **This design is closely aligned with the fundamental best layout for this shed configuration.** Any significant deviation (e.g., moving the workbench to the back wall, or placing the 60 cm bay on the left wall) would introduce the ergonomic or spatial problems described in Concepts A and C above.
