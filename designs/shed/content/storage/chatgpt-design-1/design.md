## Design Brief Summary

This proposal keeps the shed shell unchanged and designs only the internal storage fit-out for a `298 cm x 175 cm` apex-roof shed with a front wall made up of an approximately `80 cm` left window zone, `138 cm` central outward-opening double doors, and an approximately `80 cm` right window zone. The front wall remains a non-storage wall. Storage is built only on the three usable walls: the `298 cm` back wall and the two `175 cm` short walls.

The recommended layout uses:

- a full-width back-wall shelving spine built to a practical nominal depth of `42 cm`
- one rear-left deep dock at `120 cm` long x `60 cm` deep for tiles and dense awkward storage
- one rear-right `42 cm` deep workbench and tool-access wall at `115 cm` long
- shallow/open front-side strips so the windows still read as windows and the shed does not become a tunnel

Assumptions used in this design:

- The given dimensions are treated as practical internal working dimensions. Check finished inside-to-inside dimensions before cutting sheet goods.
- The `80 + 138 + 80` front-wall split is used in the plan and visual diagrams.
- The listed boxes are treated as approximate consumer storage-box groups; only the `60 x 40 x 26 cm` boxes have precise dimensions.
- Nominal `40 cm` storage is built here as `42 cm` overall depth, because a true `40 cm` box needs a little retrieval clearance and should not rub directly on cladding or framing.

## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

This shed is too shallow for one storage type to solve everything cleanly, so each usable wall gets a different job.

- The **back wall** is the main storage spine. It takes the repeatable box storage, low heavy-item shelving, paint trays, and a high rack for the `2.0 m` ladder.
- The **left short wall** becomes the single deep dock. It is `60 cm` deep for the rear `120 cm`, which is the cleanest place for the `60 x 120 cm` tiles and other dense awkward items that should stay low.
- The **right short wall** becomes the daily-use zone. It combines a `42 cm` deep workbench, open under-bench storage for the Kärcher and toolbox, and a timber-backed hanging zone for hand tools and mid-size cutters.

Why the mixed-depth arrangement is right for this shed:

- If all three storage walls were `60 cm` deep, the shed would read like a corridor.
- If all three storage walls were `40 cm` deep, the tile requirement would be solved badly.
- A single deep rear dock on one short wall solves the tile issue while keeping the centre of the shed open and credible as a standing/work area.

Clear-space result:

- Clear standing depth in front of the back shelving: about `133 cm` (`175 - 42`)
- Clear width between the rear-left dock and the rear-right bench: about `196 cm` (`298 - 60 - 42`)
- Front third of the shed feels wider than the rear because both short walls lighten up as they approach the windows and door zone

Workbench / under-bench logic:

- A modest workbench is included on the **right short wall**, the side nearer the house.
- The active tool wall sits directly above it, so the most-used tools are visible and reachable while standing at the bench.
- The heavy tile storage is deliberately kept on the opposite side. That keeps the active bench side less cluttered and avoids using the best day-to-day wall as a dead-weight storage bay.

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A: Back-Wall Box Spine

**Description**  
Use the back wall for nearly all storage and keep both short walls mostly open except for hooks and shallow rails.

**Key strengths**

- Best central openness
- Simplest build
- Very efficient for repeated storage boxes

**Key weaknesses**

- Weak solution for the `60 x 120 cm` tile requirement
- Bulky items would still end up on the floor
- No convincing bench-plus-tool-wall zone

### Concept B: Left Tile Dock + Back Spine + Right Tool Bench

**Description**  
Use the back wall for repeatable `42 cm` shelving, the rear-left short wall for a `60 cm` deep tile dock, and the rear-right short wall for a shallower workbench plus the main hanging-tool zone.

**Key strengths**

- Solves the tile requirement properly
- Preserves strong day-to-day usability
- Gives both awkward items and common tools a deliberate home

**Key weaknesses**

- More carpentry than a single shelving wall
- Slightly less raw cubic storage than a dense U-wrap

### Concept C: Rear U-Wrap Maximum Capacity

**Description**  
Use the back wall and the rear portions of both short walls as semi-tall storage, with shallow continuation toward the front.

**Key strengths**

- Highest overall storage volume
- Easy to assign categories by wall
- Strong “everything enclosed” look

**Key weaknesses**

- Highest risk of corridor feel
- More visual mass near the windows
- Less comfortable as an occasional working shed

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Selected concept: Concept B, “Left Tile Dock + Back Spine + Right Tool Bench.”**

It is the strongest option because it solves the hard parts of the brief instead of treating every item as “more shelving”:

- the tiles get a true low `60 cm` deep bay
- the back wall still works as a repeatable box-storage spine
- the right wall becomes a real daily-use station with a credible tool wall
- the heaviest awkward storage stays low and out of the active side
- the short-wall front-light rule is respected because tall/deep storage stays inside the rear `120 cm` on the left and rear `115 cm` on the right

## 11. Box-Fit Calculation

### Known `60 x 40 x 26 cm` boxes

Recommended orientation:

- `60 cm` across the shelf width
- `40 cm` into the shelf depth
- `26 cm` box height

Why the standard storage depth is set to `42 cm`, not exactly `40 cm`:

- A true `40 cm` shelf leaves no tolerance for the box lip, wall unevenness, or easy finger access.
- At `42 cm`, the boxes still behave as “40 cm deep storage” but can slide in and out without rubbing the back wall.

Per-bay fit on the back wall:

- **B1, width `82 cm`:** `1` large box per shelf
- **B2, width `134 cm`:** `2` large boxes per shelf
- **B3, width `82 cm`:** `1` large box per shelf

Total per full shelf run:

- `4` large boxes across one back-wall shelf level

If three back-wall levels are dedicated to these boxes:

- `3` levels x `4` boxes = `12` large-box positions
- Known requirement = `9` large boxes
- Spare capacity = `3` positions for growth or for medium boxes

Shelf spacing needed:

- Box height is `26 cm`
- Practical clear opening should be `29-31 cm`
- The `32 cm` shelf-pitch used between the lower back shelves gives roughly `30 cm` usable clear height after accounting for shelf thickness

### Medium and small boxes

Because exact sizes were not supplied, a practical estimate is:

- the medium consumer boxes are likely to fit best on the `128 cm` and `166 cm` back shelves
- smaller boxes should live either in the side back-wall bays or on the right-hand shallow shelves where labels remain visible

### Tile-bay capacity

- The low left dock gives a `120 cm x 60 cm` footprint
- That matches one `60 x 120 cm` tile layer exactly
- Multiple tiles can be stacked low on this deck
- If there are different tile types, use thin sacrificial boards or battens between stacks so edges do not chip

## 13. Construction Approach

Recommended structure type:

- **Hybrid freestanding timber storage**, floor-bearing first, wall-tied second

That means:

- each major unit stands on its own timber frame and transfers weight into the floor
- the shed wall only keeps the units plumb and prevents racking
- no heavy shelf should rely on thin cladding panels alone

Practical build approach:

1. Build the **back wall shelving** as a floor-standing frame with front legs, back uprights, and shelf ledgers.
2. Build the **left tile dock** as a stout box frame on a continuous plinth or closely spaced legs.
3. Build the **right bench** as a separate frame tied into the back shelving line for stiffness.
4. Screw all units back to studs / framing where available, but treat those screws as anti-tip and anti-rack fixings, not primary load paths.

Recommended timber logic:

- use `38 x 63 mm` or similar structural softwood for the main load-bearing frames
- use `25 x 50 mm` battens for shelf cleats, tool-board battens, and light rails
- double up the front rail of the tile dock if the tile stack is heavy

How to keep it strong without overengineering it:

- keep spans modest by using `3` back-wall bays rather than one giant uninterrupted shelf
- put heavy items on the lowest shelf levels only
- use sheet material as diaphragms where helpful: a plywood/OSB back panel on selected bays greatly improves stiffness
- use a continuous plinth or closely spaced legs under the tile dock instead of trusting a few long unsupported rails

## 14. Materials Recommendation

### Plywood

**Pros**

- Better screw holding at edges
- Better resistance to repeated handling and damp cycles
- Cleaner edges for exposed shelving and work surfaces

**Cons**

- Higher cost

**Best use in this design**

- workbench top
- tile-dock top and low tile deck
- front edges of the main back shelves if a cleaner finish is wanted
- tool board where hooks will be repeatedly repositioned

### OSB3

**Pros**

- Lower cost
- Structurally adequate for hidden shelves and bracing
- Good value where appearance matters less

**Cons**

- Rougher edges and surfaces
- Less pleasant for frequently touched work surfaces

**Best use in this design**

- hidden back-wall shelf decks
- back panels / bracing panels
- upper left-dock shelf where appearance is secondary

### Other sensible sheet material

**Melamine-faced chipboard / MDF** is not recommended here.  
In an unheated or semi-damp shed it is less forgiving at edges and fixings than plywood or OSB3.

### Recommended material split

- **18 mm plywood** for the workbench top, tile deck, and any shelf that will carry dense weight
- **18 mm OSB3 or plywood** for the main back-wall horizontal shelves
- **12 mm plywood or OSB3** for tool board panels, bracing panels, and lighter upper shelves

### Likely timber section sizes

- main frames: `38 x 63 mm` softwood
- light rails / cleats / battens: `25 x 50 mm`
- optional front shelf lipping: `18 x 38 mm` or similar

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. Empty the shed fully and confirm actual inside dimensions, door swing, and where structural studs / rails are available.
2. Mark the floor lines for the back shelving depth (`42 cm`), left dock depth (`60 cm`), and right bench depth (`42 cm`).
3. Level and pack the floor positions if needed; a twisted floor will make every later shelf line wrong.
4. Build and place the **back wall shelving frame** first, because it sets the datum for the whole layout.
5. Fix back-wall units lightly to the shed framing so they stay square.
6. Build the **left tile dock** and set it tight to the back shelving.
7. Build the **right bench** and tool-wall frame.
8. Add horizontal sheet decks and tops.
9. Add tool board panels, rails, hooks, and ladder brackets only after the main carcasses are fixed.
10. Load heavy items first, low down, then tune hook positions around actual tool sizes.

## 17. Safety / Access / Usability Checklist

- Heavy items remain low: tiles, toolbox, heavy tools, and Kärcher are all kept below bench height.
- Tiles are stored on a low deck, not high or overhead.
- The double-door swing is not obstructed because the front wall remains clear.
- The windows are not unnecessarily blocked because tall/deep short-wall storage stops in the rear part of each side.
- The short walls do not run as full-height deep units all the way toward the front.
- The central floor stays usable, with about `196 cm` rear clear width and about `133 cm` clear depth in front of the back shelves.
- The long ladder is secured on a high rack rather than leaning loose.
- The foldable ladder is clipped vertically rather than left on the floor.
- Frequently used tools remain visible on the right tool wall and shallow shelf.
- The BBQ has a deliberate floor pocket, so it does not drift into the central standing area.

## 19. Final Recommendation

The final recommended design is a **back-wall shelving spine with a rear-left deep tile dock and a rear-right workbench / tool wall**.

It is the best fit for this shed because it uses the full `298 cm` back wall for efficient repeatable box storage, solves the `60 cm` deep tile requirement without turning the shed into a corridor, and gives the most-used wall to the workbench and hanging-tool zone. The result stays buildable in ordinary timber and sheet goods, keeps the front wall clear, respects the window-light constraint on the short walls, and leaves a central floor area that still feels usable once the awkward items are honestly accounted for.

Practical caveats:

- check actual inside dimensions before cutting sheet goods
- if the floor is noticeably uneven, level the plinths first
- if the tile quantity is larger than expected, reinforce the tile deck with closer framing centres rather than increasing the dock height
