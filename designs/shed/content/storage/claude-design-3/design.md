## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

### Wall Roles

| Wall | Depth | Zone | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Back wall (298 cm wide) | 40 cm | Full width (298 cm) | Primary storage spine — all box groups, heavy tools, paint, garden fertiliser; 2 m ladder at high level |
| Right wall (175 cm deep) | 60 cm (rear 120 cm) | Workbench bay | Workbench surface at 90 cm; tool board (90–165 cm) above; under-bench tiles + Kärcher |
| Right wall (175 cm deep) | Open (front 55 cm) | Bulky-items parking | BBQ on floor; 1.5 m foldable ladder vertical on wall face |
| Left wall (175 cm deep) | 40 cm (rear 120 cm) | Secondary shelving | Boxes, occasional storage, Dunelm boxes |
| Left wall (175 cm deep) | Open (front 55 cm) | Garden tool zone | Long-handle tools on vertical holders; small-tool hooks |

### Why mixed depth works

The 60 × 120 cm tile format is the governing constraint. A 60 cm deep bay is the exact depth needed to store tiles flat without trimming or stacking at an angle. Combining this 60 cm bay with the workbench — the only other item in the shed that genuinely benefits from a 60 cm reach depth — makes the depth penalty earn double utility. All other storage is held to 40 cm depth, which preserves the widest possible central floor without creating an unusable corridor.

### BBQ height problem — and solution

The BBQ is 100 cm tall. A standard 90 cm workbench surface gives only 88 cm of under-bench clearance (allowing for the base frame). The BBQ **cannot** be stored under the bench without damage or impossible retrieval. Therefore the BBQ is assigned its own dedicated floor spot in the right-wall front open zone — a "bulky items parking bay" adjacent to the door. This keeps the BBQ accessible, eliminates floor clutter elsewhere, and preserves the under-bench bay for tiles and Kärcher.

### Ladder constraints

The 2 m non-foldable ladder is taller than the 190 cm eave height. Vertical storage anywhere in this shed is impossible. It therefore stores horizontally on back-wall L-brackets at 168 cm height, spanning 200 cm of the back wall (in the zone above the standard shelf run). The 1.5 m foldable ladder is 150 cm when folded — well within the 190 cm eave height — and is stored vertically on the right-wall face in the front open zone.

### Central floor space

Back-wall shelving: 40 cm deep from back wall → leaves 135 cm of clear depth from shelf face to front wall. Right-wall workbench: 60 cm deep (face at X=238), restricted to rear 120 cm only → the front 55 cm of the right wall is completely open at floor level. Left-wall shelving: 40 cm deep (face at X=40), restricted to rear 120 cm only. Resulting clear central floor: approximately **198 cm wide × 135 cm deep (~2.7 m²)** (X=40–238, Y=0–135). Both doors open outward — no interior swing intrudes.

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A — Back-Wall Dominant

**Description:** All primary storage concentrated on the 298 cm back wall as full-width, 5-level, floor-to-eave shelving at 40 cm deep. Both side walls left open or holding only minimal rear shelving (one or two shelves). No dedicated workbench. Tiles lean in a corner. Tools in boxes.

**Key strengths:**
- Maximum back-wall shelf run in a single continuous structure
- Simplest possible build — one main unit
- Maximum central floor width (~218 cm wide) since only the 40 cm back wall intrudes

**Key weaknesses:**
- No 60 cm zone: tiles cannot be stored flat; any lean-against solution is unsafe and clutters the floor
- No workbench: all DIY work on a makeshift surface or on the floor
- Tool organisation is unstructured; hand tools, cutters, and heavy tools compete for shelf space or end up in boxes
- BBQ, ladders, Kärcher, and toolbox all compete for central floor space
- Under-utilises two storage walls; total capacity is lower and layout is chaotic

---

### Concept B — Right-Bay Workbench + Back Spine + Left Secondary *(selected)*

**Description:** Right wall rear 120 cm becomes a 60 cm deep workbench bay (X=238–298). Under bench: tiles flat, Kärcher on tile stack. Tool board on right wall face (90–165 cm). Back wall: 40 cm deep shelving 238 cm wide (stopping at workbench corner), 4 shelf levels + ladder zone at high level. Back-right corner above bench: 2 small shelves. Left wall rear 120 cm: 40 cm deep shelving, 4 levels. Right front open zone: BBQ on floor + 1.5 m foldable ladder on wall. Left front zone: long-handle garden tool holders.

**Key strengths:**
- Tile storage resolved exactly: 60 cm bay depth equals tile width
- Workbench ergonomics: tool board directly in front of the user at bench
- Back wall: uniform 40 cm depth preserves maximum central floor width
- Three spatially distinct functional zones: work/tools, box/general, garden tools
- BBQ explicitly parked in the right front zone — not a floor clutter problem
- Both ladders properly stored without claiming central floor space

**Key weaknesses:**
- Workbench bay (60 cm deep) reduces right-wall shelf capacity vs flat shelving
- Under-bench retrieval of tiles requires bending — acceptable given infrequent use

---

### Concept C — Left-Bay Workbench + Back Spine + Right Secondary

**Description:** Same as Concept B but mirrored: 60 cm deep workbench on the **left** wall rear 120 cm instead of the right. Right wall rear 120 cm becomes 40 cm deep secondary shelving. Left front zone becomes the bulky-items parking area.

**Key strengths:**
- Functionally equivalent to Concept B in storage capacity
- Could suit a preference for working on the left side of the shed (slightly more natural light from the left if the house is on the right)

**Key weaknesses:**
- The right wall (closer to the house) is the natural first point of call when entering — a workbench here benefits from proximity; a secondary shelf unit on the right loses this advantage
- Long-handle garden tools on the right front zone (nearest door) is more convenient for garden access if the garden is to the right
- No fundamental structural or capacity advantage over Concept B; the right-wall workbench position is more ergonomically natural for most right-handed users

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Selected: Concept B — Right-Bay Workbench + Back Spine + Left Secondary**

Reasons:

1. **Tile storage is fully solved.** The 60 cm bay depth equals the tile short dimension exactly. Tiles lie flat under the bench on the floor, safe and retrievable.
2. **The BBQ problem is explicitly resolved.** By acknowledging that the BBQ (100 cm tall) cannot fit under a 90 cm workbench, the design assigns the right-front open zone as a dedicated bulky-items parking bay. There is no floor clutter risk.
3. **The workbench is the ergonomic hub.** Tool board faces the user at bench. Frequently used hand tools and cutters are within arm's reach. The under-bench bay holds the three items (tiles, Kärcher, toolbox) that are occasionally needed but should not clutter the floor.
4. **Back wall is the storage spine.** 238 cm of 40 cm deep shelving (stopping where the workbench occupies the right corner) with 4 levels holds all 26 boxes with capacity to spare, plus the toolbox, paint tins, garden fertiliser, and extension cord. The back-right corner (60 cm wide above bench height) adds two more small shelves. The 2 m ladder stored at high level on back-wall brackets occupies no floor space.
5. **Both side walls serve defined purposes.** Left rear: secondary box/general storage. Right rear: workbench. Left front: garden tools. Right front: BBQ + foldable ladder. Nothing is left unplanned.
6. **Central floor area remains practical.** Approximately 195 cm wide × 120 cm deep — enough to stand, work, and manoeuvre bulky items without the shed feeling narrow.

The BBQ is approximately 100 cm tall. The under-bench clearance is 88 cm. The BBQ cannot be placed under the workbench without forced compression of the kettle lid/handle, which risks damage. Yes. The tile bay requires exactly 60 cm depth, which the workbench bay provides. No other zone in this shed has 60 cm of available depth without significantly reducing the central floor. ## 13. Construction Approach

### Structural strategy: freestanding hybrid frames

Shed wall cladding is too thin to carry heavy shelving loads by anchor bolts alone. All storage units are built as **self-supporting frames** that are stabilised against racking by light fixing to the shed wall structure — specifically to the wall plates, studs, or top rail, **not** to the cladding planks themselves.

### Back wall shelving unit

- **4 floor-to-ceiling uprights** of 47×47 mm (or 44×44 mm) CLS timber, spaced to create 4 bays of ~74 cm each
- Uprights sit on the floor and are lightly screwed through the back wall into the wall plate or stud at the top — this resists forward tipping only; the floor provides the vertical load
- **Shelf boards:** 18 mm plywood or OSB, cut to 74 cm × 40 cm per bay per level
- Shelf boards rest in **timber ledger strips** (25×25 mm PAR softwood) screwed horizontally to each upright at the desired heights
- **Top frame:** a horizontal 47×47 mm timber rail connecting all uprights at the top, secured lightly to back wall
- **2 m ladder brackets:** two 75 mm-projection L-brackets (50×50×3 mm steel angle) bolted to the uprights at Z=168 cm, X=5 and X=205 cm. Each bracket must include an **upturned lip or a retaining strap** (e.g., 25 mm webbing strap with a buckle) to prevent the ladder from sliding or falling — plain flat brackets are not sufficient for overhead ladder storage

### Left wall shelving unit

- Same construction as back wall but only 120 cm long (rear section)
- 3 uprights: one at back (Y=175), one at Y=115, one at Y=55
- Stabilised by a top rail light-fixed to left wall plate
- Garden tool zone (front 55 cm) needs only a horizontal timber rail at Z=145 cm for hanging long-handle tool sockets

### Right wall workbench unit

- **Frame:** 47×47 mm CLS timber legs (4 legs: back-left, back-right, front-left, front-right corner of 120 cm × 60 cm plan)
- Legs at Z=88 cm top (bench surface will add 2 cm = 90 cm total bench height)
- Horizontal rails at 88 cm front and back connecting legs, and side rails
- **Bench surface:** 18 mm plywood × 2 layers (36 mm total) or single 25 mm structural grade plywood; glued and screwed
- Top frame at 88 cm screwed lightly into right wall through the top plate or stud to resist racking
- **Under-bench: open bay** — no shelf boards, no crossbars at floor level that would obstruct tile sliding
- **Tool board panel:** 12 mm plywood sheet, 120 cm wide × 73 cm tall, screwed to right wall face using 50 mm battens as standoffs; hooks and rails into the plywood face

### Finish recommendations

- Sand all cut shelf edges; apply one coat Danish oil or shed-grade wood preservative to timber frame and shelf boards
- Apply two coats patio/shed paint to the plywood tool board panel
- Use zinc-plated screws (50–80 mm) throughout for corrosion resistance in a garden shed environment

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. **Clear the shed** and take final measurements. Confirm all dimensions on site — especially that the 175 cm depth and 298 cm width are accurate. Check for any wall plate or stud positions.
2. **Cut all timber to length** (uprights, rails, ledger strips). Label each piece.
3. **Build the back wall shelving unit first** (it is the largest and most independent unit):
   - Lay uprights on the floor, attach ledger strips at marked heights
   - Stand uprights and connect with top rail
   - Check plumb and square; add temporary diagonal brace
   - Screw top rail lightly to back wall plate (for anti-rack fixing only)
   - Cut and fit shelf boards bay by bay
4. **Install the back-wall ladder brackets** (L-brackets at Z=168 cm on two uprights)
5. **Build the left wall shelving unit** (same process, shorter run):
   - Assemble frame, fit ledger strips, lift into place, light-fix to left wall plate
   - Fit shelf boards
6. **Install the left-front garden tool rail** (single horizontal rail at Z=145 cm on left wall face)
7. **Build the workbench unit**:
   - Assemble the base frame (4 legs + 4 rails at Z=88 cm)
   - Check level — floor may not be perfectly level; use shims if needed
   - Fix lightly to right wall through the top of the frame
   - Glue and screw two layers of 18 mm plywood for the bench surface
8. **Install the tool board**:
   - Cut plywood panel to 120×73 cm
   - Prime and paint the face
   - Attach standoff battens to the back of the panel
   - Screw panel to right wall using battens as standoffs (creates a flat surface for hooks)
9. **Install corner shelves** above the bench (back-right corner)
10. **Fit all hooks, rails, and hardware** — tool board S-hooks, ladder brackets, BBQ hook, garden tool holders
11. **Apply finish** to all timber and shelving surfaces (Danish oil or preservative)
12. **Load and organise**: tiles first (under bench), then heavy floor items, then boxes, then hanging tools

## 19. Final Recommendation

### Selected design: Concept B — Right-Bay Workbench + Back Spine + Left Secondary

This design solves the brief's five hardest constraints simultaneously:

1. **Tiles** — stored flat under the workbench at the exact required 60 cm depth; no improvised lean, no trip hazard.
2. **BBQ** — explicitly acknowledged as too tall for under-bench storage; assigned a proper dedicated floor bay in the right-front zone; wall hook prevents rolling.
3. **2 m ladder** — acknowledged as exceeding the 190 cm eave height; stored horizontally on bolted back-wall brackets; no floor space claimed.
4. **Tool access** — the tool board directly in front of the workbench user puts every frequently used hand tool and cutter within arm's reach; no digging through boxes.
5. **Box capacity** — 26 boxes of six different sizes are all allocated to specific shelf bays with quantified fit checks; the back wall and left wall have residual capacity after all boxes are placed.

The design is straightforward to build using standard CLS timber and structural plywood, with no complex joinery. It can be built over two weekends by a competent DIY enthusiast. The total material cost is estimated at £350–£500.

### Practical caveats

- Confirm all on-site dimensions before cutting — shed footprints sometimes vary slightly from stated values.
- The workbench base frame should be checked for level before fixing; the floor may need shimming.
- The 2 m ladder L-brackets must be fixed to the back wall frame uprights (not just to cladding) using appropriate bolts or coach screws.
- Tiles should be stored on a piece of 6 mm foam or rubber mat to prevent scratching the plywood floor surface.
- If the toolbox is heavier than approximately 20 kg, consider adding a low castor platform so it can be rolled out from under the bench without lifting.
