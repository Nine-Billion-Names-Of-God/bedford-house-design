### 2. Overall Storage Strategy

The design uses a **four-wall perimeter approach**, treating the shed's walls as a continuous storage spine while preserving a clear central floor. Each wall section has a defined role:

**Back wall — Primary Storage Spine (298cm):**
The back wall is divided into two distinct zones at the 160cm mark.
- *Left section (0–160cm, 40cm deep):* High-density floor-to-ceiling shelving for boxes, paints, fertiliser, and general storage. Five usable levels from floor to 1.7m, with the 2m non-foldable ladder mounted horizontally on wall brackets above.
- *Right section (160–298cm = 138cm wide, 60cm deep):* Workbench corner. The bench sits at 85cm height with a 60cm-deep × 138cm-wide work surface. Under the bench accommodates tiles (flat), the Kärcher pressure washer, and the large toolbox. A single shelf at 1.7m above the bench provides additional tool and paint storage.

**Right short wall — Workbench-Adjacent Tool Zone (175cm gable):**
- *Rear 1.2m:* 40cm-deep full-height shelving with a dedicated tool board/hanging rail at 85–160cm height. Standing at the back-wall workbench, tools on this wall are within easy arm's reach — this is the **key ergonomic win of Design-2**.
- *Front 55cm:* Open zone storing the 1.5m foldable ladder vertically and the BBQ.

**Left short wall — Garden Tool Zone (175cm gable):**
- *Rear 1.2m:* 40cm-deep full-height shelving for heavy garden items, storage boxes, fertiliser. Wall hooks above the top shelf for extension cords and ropes.
- *Front 55cm:* Open zone with long-handled tool hooks (spades, rakes, forks, brooms) mounted on the wall face.

**Key design decision — workbench on back wall (Design-2 vs Design-1):**
Design-1 placed the workbench on the right short wall rear zone. Design-2 moves it to the back wall right section. This change delivers three improvements:
1. The workbench is now on the longer, more structurally stable back wall, with more width (138cm vs ~120cm).
2. The right short wall's full 120cm rear zone is freed for the tool board and shelving — standing at the bench you turn 90° and every hand tool is visible and reachable.
3. The 60cm bench depth perfectly accommodates 60×120cm tiles stored flat underneath — tile length (120cm) fits within the 138cm bench width, tile depth (60cm) equals the bench depth exactly.

**Clear floor area:**
With 40cm-deep shelving on both short walls and 40–60cm on the back wall, the clear floor is approximately 95cm wide (175 – 40 – 40 = 95cm between side-wall shelf faces) and approximately 135cm deep (175 – 40cm back shelf). The back-right workbench zone (60cm deep, 138cm wide) slightly reduces the clear area in that corner but does not obstruct the main central space.

### 3. Layout Concept Options

**Concept A: Back-Wall Dominant (Shelving Only)**

All four storage walls fitted with uniform 40cm-deep shelving throughout. No dedicated workbench surface.

- *Configuration:* Back wall 0–298cm with 40cm shelving; both short walls rear 1.2m with 40cm shelving; front open zones unaltered.
- *Pros:* Maximum shelf and box storage capacity (~30+ boxes across back and side walls), simple and uniform construction, lower cost.
- *Cons:* No dedicated work surface for DIY or gardening prep; tiles difficult to store safely (can't slide flat under a bench); no organised tool hanging; poor ergonomics for working in shed.
- *Suitable for:* Pure passive box storage with no active DIY use of the shed.

**Concept B (RECOMMENDED): Back-Wall Right Workbench Corner**

The recommended layout. Back wall left section retains deep shelving; back wall right section becomes a 60cm-deep workbench zone.

- *Configuration:* Back wall left (0–160cm): 40cm shelving, 5 shelf levels. Back wall right (160–298cm): 60cm-deep workbench at 85cm, under-bench storage, shelf at 170cm. Right short wall rear: 40cm shelving + tool board at 85–160cm. Left short wall rear: 40cm shelving. Front open zones for ladders, BBQ, long-handle tools.
- *Pros:* Workbench on the back wall (most stable, longest wall), tiles neatly under bench, tool board adjacent to bench creates an L-shaped ergonomic work zone, clear central floor retained, non-foldable ladder stored safely at 1.7m, foldable ladder in right front zone near door.
- *Cons:* Slightly less box capacity than Concept A (approx 4 fewer boxes), marginally higher build complexity and cost due to workbench construction.

**Concept C: Distributed Dual Workbench**

Two work surfaces — the back-wall workbench of Concept B, plus a second smaller bench on the left short wall rear zone.

- *Configuration:* All of Concept B plus a 60cm-deep bench (120cm wide) on the left short wall rear zone replacing the shelving there.
- *Pros:* Two distinct work surfaces — one for power tools/heavy work, one for potting/lighter tasks; very organised shed layout.
- *Cons:* Significantly reduces box storage capacity (loses ~8 boxes from left short wall shelving); overkill for typical garden shed use; higher material and build cost; left front open zone becomes more cluttered without shelving above it.

### 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Concept B is recommended** for the following reasons:

1. **Ergonomic work zone:** The workbench on the back wall right section, combined with the tool board on the adjacent right short wall, creates a natural L-shaped work corner. Standing at the bench, you can reach the tool board with a simple 90° turn. This integration would not be possible if the workbench were on the right short wall (Design-1 arrangement).

2. **Tile storage solved elegantly:** The 60cm bench depth is not arbitrary — it exactly matches the short dimension of the 60×120cm tiles. Tiles slide in flat under the bench; the 120cm tile length is accommodated within the 138cm bench width. The tiles are heavy (300kg+ combined) and best stored at floor level without vertical stacking risks.

3. **Ladder storage hierarchy:** The 2m non-foldable ladder is mounted horizontally on the back wall at 1.7m on heavy-duty brackets spanning x=0 to x=200cm — above the top shelf, using otherwise wasted ceiling-zone space. The 1.5m foldable ladder stands vertically in the right front open zone, close to the door for easy grab-and-go access.

4. **BBQ accessible:** Stored in the right front open zone at floor level. No shelving overhead in this 55cm zone, so the BBQ can be wheeled or lifted straight out when the doors open. At 60cm diameter × 1m tall, it fits within the 55cm × 40cm open zone (BBQ is round, not square, so it fits diagonally if needed).

5. **Box storage capacity matches need:** With ~12 boxes on the back wall left section, ~8 on the left short wall, and ~6 on the right short wall lower shelves, the total reaches 26 boxes — exactly matching the stated requirement.

6. **Build priority clear:** Workbench can be built first as a freestanding unit, then pushed into position. Shelving is wall-mounted second. This allows incremental building over weekends.

### 11. Box-Fit Calculation

**Back wall left section (160cm wide × 40cm deep, shelves at 30, 65, 100, 140cm):**

Standard 60×40cm boxes are oriented with the 60cm dimension along the wall width and 40cm dimension into the shelf depth (40cm = exact depth match). Per shelf:
- 160 ÷ 60 = 2 full boxes (120cm used) + 40cm remainder
- The 40cm remainder accommodates one IKEA Samla (56×36cm, need 56cm — doesn't quite fit full width) or a smaller 40×40cm box
- Conservative count: **2 standard boxes per shelf level**
- 4 shelf levels × 2 = **8 standard boxes on back wall left**
- Plus 4 smaller/IKEA boxes in the 40cm gap slots = **12 total on back wall left**

### 16. Practical Build Sequence

Building in this sequence minimises the need to rearrange components and allows each stage to be independently completed in a single work session:

1. **Clear the shed completely and clean the floor.** Measure actual internal dimensions — confirm 298cm width and 175cm depth (sheds can vary slightly from stated sizes). Mark any discrepancies on a sketch.

2. **Mark all shelf heights on the wall surfaces** using a spirit level and pencil. Mark: 30cm, 65/70cm, 85cm (bench height reference), 100cm, 120cm, 140cm, 160cm, 170cm, and 190cm (eave). Also mark the x=160cm back wall divider line.

3. **Install back wall left section vertical uprights.** Locate shed wall studs/posts behind cladding with a stud finder. Fix 2× full-height 47×100mm uprights with M10 coach bolts at x=5cm and x=157cm (leaving 3cm margin at corner and before the workbench zone).

4. **Install back wall left section shelf bearers and boards, bottom to top.** Start at floor level, fit bearer pairs at 30, 65, 100, 140cm. Lay OSB3 shelf boards. Working bottom to top means each completed shelf provides a useful work platform for fitting the next.

5. **Install ladder brackets at 1.7m on back wall.** Fix 2× heavy-duty horizontal brackets at x=15cm and x=185cm, height=170cm, using M10 bolts into wall structure. Test load each bracket before mounting the ladder.

6. **Build the workbench frame off-site (garage/driveway).** Cut all timber, assemble legs and apron rails with 75mm structural screws and M10 bolts at corners. Attach workbench top. This is easier to build flat on the ground or on trestles.

7. **Move the assembled workbench into the shed** and push it into the back-right corner (x=160cm to x=298cm, against the back wall). Level with plastic packers if needed. Anchor the rear apron rail to the back wall with 2× M10 coach bolts.

8. **Install the above-bench shelf at 170cm.** Fix 2× horizontal 47×100mm bearers to the back wall at 170cm height within the right section (x=162–296cm). Lay and screw the 138×40cm plywood shelf board. Add 2 front L-brackets for additional support.

9. **Install right short wall rear shelving uprights** at x=55cm and x=173cm on the right wall. Fix with coach bolts. Install shelf bearers at 30, 70, 160cm (three shelf levels around the tool board zone).

10. **Install the tool board on the right short wall.** Position 120×80cm birch ply panel at 85–160cm height, covering the rear zone wall face. Screw to wall through uprights. Fix French-cleat rails at 90cm and 135cm heights for hook flexibility.

11. **Install right short wall shelf boards** at 30cm, 70cm, and 160cm. The tool board occupies 85–160cm, so these three shelf levels provide storage below and above the tool board.

12. **Install left short wall rear shelving uprights and shelf boards** (4 shelf levels at 30, 70, 120, 160cm). Follow same process as right short wall but without tool board. Install wall hooks above top shelf for extension cords (use screw-in cup hooks).

13. **Install long-handle tool hooks in left front open zone.** Fix 5–6 heavy-duty screw-in hooks or a single rail with individual hooks at approximately 120cm height on the left wall face, in the 0–55cm zone.

14. **Load items in correct sequence:** Tiles first (heaviest — slide under workbench before the space is surrounded by other items). Then: heavy floor-level items on lower shelf zones. Then: ascending by weight — heavier items on lower shelves, lighter items higher. Paints at 65cm (comfortable lifting). Boxes filling shelves. IKEA Samla boxes at 140cm.

15. **Mount the non-foldable ladder on back wall brackets at 1.7m.** Lift into brackets. Secure with bungee cord through both brackets to prevent ladder sliding off.

16. **Store foldable ladder vertically in right front open zone** (leaned against wall, folded, secured with one hook-and-eye at 120cm height if desired).

### 19. Final Recommendation

**Design-2 (Concept B) is the right solution for this shed.** The single most important design decision here is the placement of the workbench: on the back wall's right section (Design-2) rather than the right short wall's rear zone (Design-1). This seemingly small change unlocks a fundamentally better shed ergonomics.

**The L-shaped work zone:** When the workbench occupies the back wall right section (x=160–298cm, 60cm deep), and the tool board occupies the right short wall rear zone (85–160cm height), these two features form a natural L-shaped work corner. Standing at the bench, you look at the back wall. Turn 90° right and every hand tool — secateurs, trowels, pruners, loppers, shears — is on the tool board at exactly the right height (85–160cm, i.e., waist to eye level). You never need to walk across the shed to retrieve a tool. This is the kind of workflow optimisation that makes a difference every time you use the shed.

**Tiles solved properly:** The 60cm × 120cm tiles require a specific solution. Storing them vertically is unstable and potentially dangerous. Storing them flat in a stack requires a flat horizontal surface at the right height. The workbench's 60cm depth and 138cm width accommodate the tiles perfectly — they slide in horizontally, the 120cm tile length sits safely within the 138cm bench width, and the floor-level position means no heavy lifting is needed to store or retrieve them. This is not a coincidence — the bench dimensions were chosen with the tile storage requirement in mind.

**Design-2 vs Design-1:** The shift is from a right-short-wall workbench (Design-1) to a back-wall workbench (Design-2). Design-1's workbench on the right short wall was 120cm wide — adequate but not ideal for tile storage (tiles are 120cm long, no margin). It also left the back wall uniformly shallow (40cm throughout), meaning the right corner of the shed was never as efficiently used. Design-2 deepens the back-right zone to 60cm for the workbench, adding real utility exactly where it's needed.

**Build recommendation:** Start with the workbench (weekend 1). It can be assembled in a garage or driveway, then moved into the shed. Once it's in place, all other measurements for shelf uprights can be taken against it. Weekend 2: back wall left shelving and right short wall shelving + tool board. Weekend 3: left short wall shelving, tool hooks, and final loading. The shed reaches usable state after weekend 1 (workbench operational) and is fully complete after weekend 3.

**Budget:** At £210–£325 for self-build materials, this is an extremely cost-effective solution. A professional shed organisation fit-out for the same specification would cost £800–£1,500+. The primary cost saving comes from using CLS structural timber and OSB3 rather than branded modular storage systems — the functional result is identical or superior due to the custom sizing to the shed's exact dimensions.

**Final note on the 26 boxes:** The calculation in Section 11 confirms all 26 boxes fit with good methodology — back wall left takes 12, left short wall takes 8, right short wall takes 6. The mix of standard 60×40cm boxes and IKEA Samla 56×36cm medium boxes is accommodated by the 40cm shelf depth throughout. If box count grows in future, the above-bench shelf and the left wall upper zone provide additional overflow capacity.
