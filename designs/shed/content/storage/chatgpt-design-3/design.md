## Design Brief Summary

This proposal keeps the shed structure unchanged and designs only the internal fit-out for a `298 cm x 175 cm` apex-roof shed with:

- a front wall treated as non-storage
- central outward-opening double doors of about `138 cm`
- window zones of about `80 cm` on each side of the doors
- a stated highest point of `217.9 cm`
- lower side-wall/eaves height of `190 cm`

Storage is used only on the three allowed walls:

- the `298 cm` back wall
- the `175 cm` left short wall
- the `175 cm` right short wall

The selected layout uses:

- a full-width back-wall shelf spine at `42 cm` finished depth
- a rear-left `120 x 60 cm` deep dock for tiles and dense awkward storage
- a rear-right `110 x 42 cm` workbench and tool-access wall
- open/shallow front-side strips so the shed still feels usable and the window zones are not visually buried

Planning assumptions:

- The quoted dimensions are treated as practical inside working dimensions. Check finished inside-to-inside sizes before cutting sheet goods.
- The roof geometry is treated as giving more usable height near the shed centre line. The floor plan does not depend on ridge direction; only the highest shelf / ladder rack location may need slight adjustment on site.
- Only the `60 x 40 x 26 cm` boxes have exact dimensions. The other box groups are treated as typical domestic medium/small storage boxes and should be spot-checked before locking final shelf spacing.
- “`40 cm` shelving” is built here as about `42 cm` finished depth because a true `40 cm` box needs a little retrieval clearance and should not scrape cladding or framing.

## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

Each usable wall gets a different job so the shed does not become a narrow U-shaped tunnel.

- The **back wall** becomes the main repeatable storage spine. It is the best location for box storage because it gives the longest uninterrupted run and does not steal width from the centre aisle.
- The **left short wall** becomes the single deep dock. Its rear `120 x 60 cm` footprint is the cleanest place for the tile requirement and other dense awkward items that should stay low and quiet.
- The **right short wall** becomes the active side. It combines a modest workbench, open under-bench storage, a tool board, and a shallow shelf/rail zone for high-frequency small and medium tools.

Why the mixed-depth approach is right:

- Making all three storage walls `60 cm` deep would make a `175 cm` deep shed feel corridor-like.
- Making all three storage walls about `40 cm` deep would be good for boxes but weak for the tiles.
- One purposeful `60 cm` dock plus mostly `42 cm` storage solves the awkward item honestly without giving away the whole floor.

How the central floor is preserved:

- Clear depth in front of the back shelving stays about `133 cm` (`175 - 42`).
- Rear clear width between the left deep dock and right bench stays about `196 cm` (`298 - 60 - 42`).
- The short-wall front zones remain open or shallow, so the front third of the shed stays readable and easy to enter.

Workbench decision:

- A workbench is included because this shed needs one active daily-use side rather than only stacked storage.
- The bench is deliberately limited to `110 cm` run length so a real front-right BBQ pocket still exists.
- The bench wall becomes the dedicated tool-access zone for secateurs, trowels, pruners, gloves, loppers, hedge shears, pruning saws, extension cord, and fertiliser.

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A: Back-Wall Shelf Spine Only

**Description**  
Use the full back wall for box storage and keep both short walls mostly open except for light hooks and rails.

**Key strengths**

- Best central openness
- Simplest carpentry
- Strong repeated box efficiency

**Key weaknesses**

- Weak answer to the `60 x 120 cm` tile problem
- Bulky items would still spill onto the floor
- No convincing workbench-plus-tool-wall zone

### Concept B: Right Deep Workbench Bay + Shallow Left Side

**Description**  
Use the rear-right short wall as the full `60 cm` deep workbench bay, keep the back wall at `42 cm`, and use the left side for shallower shelving and ladder storage.

**Key strengths**

- Excellent workbench ergonomics
- Deep under-bench storage
- Clear active side near the house

**Key weaknesses**

- The active side becomes the bulkiest side
- The front-right BBQ pocket gets squeezed
- Day-to-day movement feels tighter because the deep zone sits on the most active wall

### Concept C: Left Tile Dock + Back Spine + Right Workbench

**Description**  
Use the rear-left short wall for the single `60 cm` deep tile dock, the back wall for the repeated shelf spine, and the rear-right short wall for a shallower workbench and tool wall.

**Key strengths**

- Solves the tile requirement directly
- Keeps the active bench side shallower and easier to use
- Preserves a real front-right bulky-item pocket
- Balances capacity, access, and floor openness better than the alternatives

**Key weaknesses**

- Slightly more carpentry than a single shelving run
- Slightly less raw cubic storage than an aggressive rear U-wrap

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Selected concept: Concept C, “Left Tile Dock + Back Spine + Right Workbench.”**

This is the strongest solution because it solves the difficult parts of the brief directly rather than pretending everything is just more shelving:

- the tiles get a true low `120 x 60 cm` bay
- the back wall still works as a repeatable box-storage field
- the right wall becomes a genuine daily-use station with tool board and work surface
- the heaviest awkward loads stay low and off the active side
- the front-side portions of both short walls stay lighter, which protects the feel of the door/window end

### Final dimensional layout

- **Back wall shelf spine:** `298 cm W x 42 cm D`
- **Back shelf nominal bays:** `84 cm + 130 cm + 84 cm`
- **Rear-left tile dock:** `120 cm L x 60 cm D x 92 cm top height`
- **Left-front ladder / long-tool strip:** `55 cm` wall run, about `15-18 cm` projection only
- **Rear-right workbench:** `110 cm L x 42 cm D x 90 cm top height`
- **Right-front BBQ pocket:** `65 cm` wall run left open at floor level
- **Rear clear width between side units:** about `196 cm`
- **Clear depth in front of back shelving:** about `133 cm`

## 11. Box-Fit Calculation

### Known large boxes: `60 x 40 x 26 cm` (9 units)

Recommended orientation on the standard shelves:

- `60 cm` dimension across shelf width
- `40 cm` dimension front-to-back
- allow about `1-2 cm` retrieval clearance at the rear and a small front lip

Why `42 cm` depth is appropriate:

- A true `40 cm` finished shelf is too tight for a `40 cm` deep box once wall unevenness, front lipping, and hand clearance are considered.
- A `42 cm` finished shelf gives the box a workable fit without wasting much central floor area.

Practical per-shelf capacity using the nominal bay widths:

- **B1 (`84 cm` nominal, about `78-80 cm` practical clear):** `1` large box
- **B2 (`130 cm` nominal, about `124-126 cm` practical clear):** `2` large boxes
- **B3 (`84 cm` nominal, about `78-80 cm` practical clear):** `1` large box

So one full back shelf run stores about **4 large boxes**.

Recommended shelf spacing for the large boxes:

- Box height is `26 cm`
- Allow `2.5-4 cm` clearance for hand access and lid movement
- Target clear shelf opening: about `29-30 cm`

The proposed back-shelf stack gives three good box levels:

- `54 cm` shelf
- `90 cm` shelf
- `126 cm` shelf

That means:

- **3 box-ready shelves x 4 boxes per full run = 12 large-box positions**
- Known need is **9 large boxes**
- Result: **3 spare large-box positions** for overflow or easier spacing

### Other box groups

Because exact dimensions are not supplied, these are approximate planning fits:

- Typical **medium domestic boxes** around `38-40 cm` wide can fit:
  - about `2` per side bay shelf
  - about `3` per centre bay shelf
- Typical **small boxes** can fit:
  - about `3-4` per side bay shelf
  - about `5-6` per centre bay shelf

Storage efficiency conclusion:

- The back wall alone has enough linear shelf to absorb all **9 large boxes** plus the known medium and small box groups, while still reserving the lowest deck for heavy items and keeping the bench/tool wall free for active use.

## 13. Construction Approach

Recommended structural strategy:

- **floor-bearing hybrid construction**
- freestanding timber frames carrying the load to the floor
- light wall fixings only to prevent sway and racking

Why this is the right approach:

- Thin shed walls should not be asked to carry heavy shelves full of boxes, paint, and tools.
- Freestanding ladder-frame shelving keeps the load path simple and honest.
- A few screws into the shed studs or added wall battens are still useful to keep the whole fit-out square.

Suggested framing approach:

- Use `38 x 63 mm` or `44 x 69 mm` timber for the main structural frames.
- Build the back wall as a run of upright ladder frames at about `60-65 cm` centres.
- Build the left dock and right bench as separate carcass-like frames with full-height legs down to the floor.
- Use `38 x 38 mm` or similar as shelf cleats, front lips, small rails, and back battens.
- Add a `12 mm` plywood backboard only where it adds function, especially at the tool board. Full backing is not required everywhere.

Strength without overengineering:

- Keep spans modest.
- Put uprights under shelf breaks rather than chasing long unsupported spans.
- Add front nosing / lipping to shelves carrying boxes or heavy tools.
- Tie each unit back to wall studs in a few places, but do not rely on cladding alone.

## 14. Materials Recommendation

### Plywood

**Pros**

- Stronger and stiffer for shelf edges and workbench tops
- Better screw holding
- Better in occasional damp than MDF
- Cleaner edges if some surfaces remain visible

**Cons**

- More expensive than OSB

**Best use in this design**

- workbench top
- any heavily loaded shelf decks
- tool board
- front shelf lipping / exposed parts

### OSB3

**Pros**

- Lower cost
- Good structural performance when fully supported
- Fine for hidden carcass panels and lower-sensitivity shelf decks

**Cons**

- Rougher edges and appearance
- Less pleasant for tool-board use
- Not as nice for a bench top unless overlaid

**Best use in this design**

- hidden shelf decks if front-lipped and well-supported
- dock tops or side panels in utility areas
- non-visual internal dividers

### Other sensible sheet material

- **Hardboard / thin sacrificial skin:** good as a replaceable top skin on the workbench
- **MDF:** not recommended for a shed environment because damp tolerance is poor

### Recommended mix for this design

- `18 mm plywood` for the workbench top, tool board, and any shelf spans carrying dense loads
- `18 mm plywood` or `18 mm OSB3` for standard box shelves
- `12 mm plywood` for dividers, backers, and local backing where needed

Likely timber sections:

- main framing: `38 x 63 mm` or `44 x 69 mm`
- shelf lips / cleats / rails: `38 x 38 mm`
- optional tool strips / stop battens: `18 x 44 mm` or similar

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. Empty the shed and confirm real inside dimensions, wall stud positions, and floor flatness.
2. Mark the non-storage front wall and set the rear limit lines for the `42 cm`, `60 cm`, and open-pocket zones.
3. Build and level the back-wall shelf frame first because it defines the room.
4. Fix the back frame lightly to studs / battens for anti-rack stability.
5. Build the left `120 x 60 cm` tile dock and level it to the back unit.
6. Build the right `110 x 42 cm` workbench and check that the remaining front-right BBQ pocket is genuinely usable.
7. Fit shelf decks, bench top, dock top, and any upper shelf panels.
8. Add the tool board, shallow shelf, cutter rail, ladder clips, and long-tool strip.
9. Add front lips / retaining battens on heavy or frequently bumped shelves.
10. Load the heaviest items first, then box-test shelf spacing before final hook placement.
11. Label the recurring box runs once the final contents settle.

## 17. Safety / Access / Usability Checklist

- Heavy items are kept low on the back low deck, under the bench, or on the tile dock.
- Tiles are stored low in the only true deep bay and not overhead.
- The double-door swing remains clear because the front wall is still treated as non-storage.
- Windows are not boxed in by deep/tall storage at the front ends of the short walls.
- Tall/deep short-wall storage stays inside the rear `120 cm` limit.
- The central floor remains usable with about `196 cm` rear clear width and `133 cm` clear depth in front of the back shelves.
- The `2.0 m` ladder is bracketed securely on the back wall, not leaned loose.
- The foldable ladder is clipped to a shallow strip, not left on the floor.
- Frequently used tools stay between about `95 cm` and `168 cm` on the tool wall for easy reach.
- Heavy hand tools are not stored high overhead where they could fall.
- The BBQ has a dedicated floor pocket rather than blocking the bench or door zone.

## 19. Final Recommendation

The final recommendation is a **split-duty three-wall layout**:

- full-width `42 cm` back-wall shelving
- rear-left `120 x 60 cm` tile dock
- rear-right `110 x 42 cm` workbench and tool wall
- open front-side pockets for the foldable ladder and BBQ

It is the best option because it solves the hardest item types honestly while keeping the centre of the shed open enough to move, sort, and occasionally work inside. The main caveat is that the exact upper shelf / ladder-rack height should be checked on site against the actual roof framing before cutting the highest pieces.
