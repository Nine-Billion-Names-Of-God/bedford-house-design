## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

### Wall roles

| Wall | Role | Depth | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Back wall (298 cm) | Primary box and general storage spine | 40 cm | Full-width, floor-to-eave shelving; right-corner tool board |
| Right side wall (175 cm) | Deep workbench bay + tool-access wall + front open zone | 60 cm (rear 120 cm) | Workbench at 85 cm; tiles + Kärcher under bench; tool board above bench |
| Left side wall (175 cm) | Secondary shelving + long-handle tool zone | 40 cm (rear 120 cm) | Front 55 cm: long-handle garden tools on hooks |

### Why mixed depth is the right approach

The tile format (60 × 120 cm) **demands** a 60 cm deep bay somewhere. Placing the 60 cm deep zone on the right side wall combined with the workbench concentrates bulk storage near one corner and leaves the full back wall at only 40 cm deep — keeping the central floor clear. Using 40 cm elsewhere is the maximum practical depth without visually narrowing the shed.

### How central floor space is preserved

With the back wall at 40 cm, left wall at 40 cm, and right wall storage limited to the rear 120 cm section, the unobstructed central floor measures approximately **198 cm wide × 135 cm deep** — just over half the total floor area (2.7 m²). Both double doors open outward so the entry zone is unobstructed.

### Workbench and under-bench zone

A workbench is included on the **right side wall, rear section** (120 cm long × 60 cm deep × 85 cm high). Under the bench: tiles stored horizontally on the floor (60 cm deep exactly matches tile depth), Kärcher pressure washer, and rolling toolbox. Above the bench on the right wall face: a **tool board** with hooks, rails and pegs from 85 cm to 160 cm height, plus one shelf at 165 cm.

### Tool-access wall

The tool board is mounted directly on the **right side wall** above the workbench. Standing at the bench, all frequently used hand tools and mid-size cutters are at eye level within arm's reach. This is the primary ergonomic advantage of this design.

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A — Back Wall Dominant

**Description:** All primary storage concentrated on the back wall as deep, full-width floor-to-ceiling shelving (5–6 levels). Both side walls use minimal rear-section shelving only. No dedicated workbench; tiles stored leaning against one side wall. Tools hung loosely on one side wall.

**Strengths:**
- Maximum total shelf linear footage
- Simple to design and build
- Easy to understand and use

**Weaknesses:**
- No 60 cm deep zone — tiles are awkward without a proper bay
- No workbench means no comfortable working surface
- Long-handle tools and small hand tools all compete for hooks on side walls
- No clear hierarchy between item types

---

### Concept B — Corner Workbench (Right Side Bay) + Back Wall Shelving *(selected)*

**Description:** Right side wall rear section becomes a 60 cm deep workbench bay. Under bench: tiles, Kärcher. Tool board on right wall face above bench. Back wall: full-width 40 cm shelving. Left side wall: 40 cm shelving (rear 120 cm) + long-handle tool hooks (front 55 cm). Right front zone: BBQ + foldable ladder.

**Strengths:**
- 60 cm deep zone satisfies tile + Kärcher + toolbox storage in one logical bay
- Tool board directly in front of workbench — ergonomically ideal
- Back wall remains 40 cm throughout — maximises central floor width
- Clear functional zones: work / store / garden tools
- BBQ and foldable ladder neatly contained in front open zone of right wall

**Weaknesses:**
- Workbench reduces total right-wall shelf capacity
- Under-bench access requires bending — acceptable for tiles (infrequently moved)

---

### Concept C — Back Wall Workbench + Side Wall Shelving

**Description:** Workbench positioned at the back wall right section (~120 cm wide), extending 60 cm into the shed. Back wall left section: 40 cm shelving. Both side walls: full-rear shelving at 40 cm. Tool board on right side wall adjacent to back-wall workbench.

**Strengths:**
- Workbench on the longer, stiffer back wall
- Slightly more workbench width (120 cm vs workbench in Concept B's 120 cm — same)

**Weaknesses:**
- Back wall with 60 cm deep section means the floor is 20 cm narrower in that corner
- The right side wall tool board is no longer directly in front of the workbench — you turn 90° away from the bench to reach tools
- Less natural ergonomic flow
- Transition between 40 cm and 60 cm sections on same wall is less clean to build

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Selected: Concept B — Corner Workbench + Back Wall Shelving**

This design is the strongest solution because:

1. **The 60 cm tile constraint is resolved cleanly.** Tiles store flat under the workbench in a bay that is exactly the right depth (60 cm = tile width).
2. **The workbench and tool board form a coherent workstation.** Standing at the bench, the tool board is directly in front — the most ergonomically efficient arrangement possible.
3. **The back wall remains 40 cm deep throughout**, preserving an additional 20 cm of central floor width compared to a 60 cm back-wall bench configuration.
4. **Three clear zones** emerge naturally: work zone (right rear), box and general storage (back wall + left rear), garden tool access (left front and right-wall tool board).
5. **The BBQ and foldable ladder have a logical home** in the right front open zone without disrupting the central aisle.
6. **Long-handle garden tools** (spades, rakes, forks, brooms) are stored on the left front wall face — visible, accessible, out of the main aisle.

## 13. Construction Approach

### Structural philosophy

The shed walls are thin timber-framed cladding. They cannot support heavy point loads from shelf brackets alone. The storage units should be **freestanding A-frames** for each wall bay, with light fixings back to the wall only as a **stability tiebacks** (anti-tip braces), not load-carriers.

### Back wall shelving unit

- **Two freestanding ladder frames** (uprights + cross rails) standing ~30 cm from the back wall, positioned at regular intervals
- Shelf boards (18 mm plywood or 18 mm OSB) spanning between frames
- Uprights: 47 × 75 mm (2 × 3 in) CLS timber — 5 uprights across 238 cm span (one at each end + 3 intermediate at ~50 cm spacing)
- Top rail and base rail tie the uprights into a rigid frame
- Light screwed batten into wall studs at the top rail for anti-tip
- Shelves sit in dadoes or on 47 × 47 mm cleats screwed to uprights

### Right wall workbench + bay

- **Legs:** 4 × 75 × 75 mm posts (or 47 × 100 mm), set at corners and mid-span of 120 cm bench
- **Bench top:** 25 mm plywood (or 2 × 18 mm layers) glued and screwed — provides solid work surface
- **Under-bench sides:** open or fitted with 18 mm plywood side panels with low retaining rail to stop tiles shifting
- **Top shelf:** 18 mm plywood on 47 × 47 mm cleats screwed to uprights and back wall
- **Tool board:** 12 mm plywood panel screwed to right wall studs with 4–6 fixings

### Left wall shelving

- Same ladder-frame approach as back wall
- Shallower (40 cm) so only 2 uprights needed per bay (total 3 uprights for 120 cm width)
- Light anti-tip batten at top into wall stud

### Long-handle tool zone (left front)

- Four **double-ended tool hooks** or a **galvanised tool rail** screwed into the gable wall through timber studs
- Position at 155 cm height for long-handle tools hung by head
- Pre-drill pilot holes to avoid splitting thin cladding

### Front zone (right side — BBQ + ladder)

- Foldable ladder: two **heavy-duty wall brackets** at 110 cm height, fixed into right gable wall studs
- No shelving; open floor area only

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. **Mark out the shed floor** with chalk lines showing all storage zone perimeters (back wall zone, right bay, left wall zone, open zones).

2. **Build the back wall shelving unit** (the largest, heaviest unit):
   - Cut uprights to height (190 cm)
   - Assemble each ladder frame flat on the floor (pair of uprights + cleats)
   - Stand frames in position, check plumb, temporarily brace
   - Cut and fit shelf boards
   - Fix anti-tip batten at top to wall stud
   - Pre-install ladder brackets at correct height before loading shelves

3. **Build the left wall shelving unit** (same approach, smaller scale):
   - Same ladder-frame method
   - Shorter unit (120 cm wide × 40 cm deep)
   - Fix tool hooks in left front zone before installing the shelving unit (easier access)

4. **Build the workbench + right bay:**
   - Set out and cut legs (75 × 75 mm)
   - Assemble bench apron frame
   - Fit bench top (plywood panels glued + screwed)
   - Install under-bench tile rail / retaining lip
   - Mount tool board to right wall face (screw into studs through 12 mm ply)
   - Install over-bench shelf R-A

5. **Fit right front zone accessories:**
   - Fix ladder wall brackets at 110 cm height
   - No further construction needed in this zone

6. **Load storage in recommended order:**
   - Heaviest items first (tiles under bench, fertiliser on lowest shelves)
   - Toolbox under bench
   - Ladders into brackets
   - BBQ in front zone
   - Then boxes, paints, tools

7. **Final adjustments:**
   - Check all anti-tip fixings
   - Add labels to shelf bays
   - Fit additional hooks as needed

## 19. Final Recommendation

**Selected design: Corner Workbench + Back Wall Storage Spine (Concept B)**

This design is the right solution for this shed for three reasons:

**1. The tile problem is solved perfectly.** The 60 × 120 cm tiles need exactly the 60 cm deep workbench bay provided. No other location in this shed can accommodate them as cleanly. Under the workbench, tiles are accessible (slide out from the aisle side), protected (enclosed bay, off the main floor), and do not obstruct day-to-day movement.

**2. The workbench and tool board form a genuine workstation.** Placing the tool board on the right wall face directly above the workbench means every small hand tool, mid-size cutter, and pair of gloves is within arm's reach while working. This is the highest-value use of a 120 cm × 75 cm wall area in this shed.

**3. The back wall is preserved as a clean 40 cm-deep storage spine.** All ~26 boxes, paints, fertiliser, and heavy tools are accommodated across 5 shelf levels on the back wall and left wall. The back wall's full 190 cm height is used efficiently; the non-foldable 2 m ladder stored horizontally on wall brackets uses the otherwise dead space above the top shelf.

**Practical caveats:**

- Verify the rolling toolbox height before building — if it exceeds 85 cm, use a flat-pack tool chest instead, or raise the bench to 90 cm.
- The BBQ at 60 cm diameter is slightly larger than the 55 cm front zone depth. In practice, position it with the lid handle pointing away from the wall — it will sit partly across the zone boundary without obstructing the main aisle.
- Pre-drill all pilot holes when fixing into the shed's thin wall cladding to avoid splitting.
- The tile load under the workbench may be heavy (depending on tile batch size). If storing more than 15–20 tiles, check the shed floor's ground-bearing capacity and consider a concrete slab base extension under the bench if on soft ground.
