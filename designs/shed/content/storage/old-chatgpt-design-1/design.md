## Design Brief Summary

This proposal keeps the shed shell unchanged and designs only the internal storage fit-out for a `298 cm x 175 cm` apex-roof shed with a central outward-opening door on the front wall and windows either side of the door. The front wall stays a non-storage wall. Storage is concentrated on the three usable walls only: the `298 cm` back wall plus the two `175 cm` short walls.

The recommended layout uses:

- a full-width `40 cm` deep storage spine on the back wall
- one `60 cm` deep short-wall bay for tiles and dense bulky storage
- one `40 cm` deep short-wall utility bench for day-to-day items
- shallow front-side hanging zones so the entry remains bright and open

Assumptions used in this design:

- Dimensions are treated as practical internal working dimensions. Check actual internal cladding-to-cladding sizes before cutting.
- Door width is not supplied, so the top-down sketch assumes an approximately `90 cm` central door opening for illustration only.
- Exact dimensions were only provided for the `60 x 40 x 26 cm` boxes. Other box groups are treated as mixed smaller formats unless site-measured otherwise.
- Roof geometry is interpreted as a gable on the front and back walls, with the highest point on the back wall centre at `217.9 cm` and the short walls running under `190 cm` eaves.

## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

The final design uses the three usable walls as different storage types rather than forcing one shelving pattern everywhere.

- The **back wall** becomes the main `40 cm` deep storage spine. It takes the repeated box modules, paint storage, heavy tools at low level, and a high-level rack for the `2.0 m` ladder.
- The **right short wall** becomes the single deep bay: `60 cm` deep for the first `120 cm` measured from the back wall. This is the safest place for the tile bay because the tile footprint is `60 x 120 cm`, it keeps the weight low, and it does not steal depth from the whole shed.
- The **left short wall** becomes a `40 cm` deep utility bench and under-bench zone. This gives a usable working surface without making the shed feel like floor-to-ceiling shelving on all three sides.

Why this mixed-depth approach works in this shed:

- A full `60 cm` depth everywhere would leave only `115 cm` front-to-back standing depth in front of the back wall, which would feel cramped.
- A uniform `40 cm` layout everywhere would preserve space but handle the tile requirement poorly.
- Concentrating the `60 cm` depth into only one rear side bay keeps a clear middle while still solving the densest storage problem.

Clear-space result:

- Standing depth in front of the back wall shelving remains `135 cm` (`175 - 40`).
- In the rear working zone, clear width between side units remains about `198 cm` (`298 - 60 - 40`).
- In the front zone, where only shallow hanging storage remains, clear width opens back out to roughly `268 cm`.

This avoids the “narrow corridor” problem and gives a usable standing/work zone immediately inside the door.

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A: Back-Wall Dominant Grid

**Description**  
Full-height `40 cm` shelving on the back wall, with only shallow hooks or low bins on the short walls.

**Strengths**

- Best clear central floor area
- Simplest build
- Very efficient for repeated box storage

**Weaknesses**

- No natural `60 cm` tile bay
- Bulky items would still crowd bench/floor level
- Weakest workshop usability

### Concept B: Deep Side Bay + Back Spine + Utility Bench

**Description**  
Back wall used for `40 cm` full-height shelving, one short wall used for a `60 cm` deep rear bay with tile storage under a bench, the opposite short wall used for a `40 cm` utility bench with under-bench bulky-item storage.

**Strengths**

- Solves the tile requirement cleanly
- Good mix of shelves, under-bench storage, and a practical work surface
- Preserves an open centre better than a full U-shaped shelving layout

**Weaknesses**

- More carpentry than a single wall of shelves
- Slightly less raw shelf volume than a maximum-capacity U-shape

### Concept C: Rear U-Shape Max Capacity

**Description**  
Full-height back wall plus tall or semi-tall storage for the rear `120 cm` of both short walls.

**Strengths**

- Highest cubic storage volume
- Strong visual sense of “everything has a home”

**Weaknesses**

- Most likely to feel like a corridor
- Greater chance of blocking light near the front windows
- Less comfortable as a small working shed

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Recommended concept: Concept B, “Back Spine + Deep Side Bay + Utility Bench.”**

It is the best solution because it solves all of the difficult requirements at once:

- it gives the design a real `60 cm` deep zone without making the whole shed deep-storage
- it keeps the central area open enough to stand, turn, and work
- it keeps the heaviest items low
- it places frequent-use items at bench height or in shallow wall-hung zones
- it uses the back wall centre, where roof height is most useful, for the tallest storage
- it respects the short-wall rule by keeping the deeper/taller short-wall storage to roughly the rear `120 cm`

### Final dimensional layout

- **Back wall storage spine:** `298 cm W x 40 cm D`
- **Right-wall deep bay:** `120 cm L x 60 cm D x 92 cm bench height`, with upper shelves only over that rear `120 cm`
- **Left-wall utility bench:** `130 cm L x 40 cm D x 90 cm bench height`, with shallow upper shelf / tool board over the rear `120 cm`
- **Right front open strip:** `55 cm` long, shallow hanging storage only
- **Left front open strip:** `45 cm` long, shallow hanging storage only

## 11. Box-Fit Calculation

### Known governing box size

- Known box footprint: `60 x 40 x 26 cm`
- Shelf depth target for “40 cm” shelves should be built as **about `41-42 cm` finished depth** rather than a literal zero-clearance `40 cm`

That allows:

- `40 cm` box depth to sit fully on the shelf
- a small back stop or front lip
- tolerance for box handles and wall irregularity

### Back-wall box capacity

Back wall width is `298 cm`. This design uses practical shelf modules of:

- `72 cm` left bay
- `150 cm` centre bay
- `72 cm` right bay
- about `4 cm` total scribe / build tolerance

Box fit per shelf run:

- B1 (`72 cm`): `1` box
- B2 (`150 cm`): `2` boxes
- B3 (`72 cm`): `1` box

So one full-width shelf run stores **`4` of the `60 x 40` boxes**.

If three shelf levels are allocated to boxes:

- `4 boxes x 3 levels = 12 box positions`

That comfortably covers the known `9` large `60 x 40 x 26` boxes and leaves `3` spare positions for mixed containers.

### Shelf spacing

For `26 cm` high boxes:

- minimum usable clear opening: about `29 cm`
- preferred clear opening: about `31-32 cm`

Recommended back-wall shelf clear heights:

- lower opening: `32 cm`
- middle opening: `32-35 cm`
- upper opening: `32-35 cm`

### Depth check

| Shelf depth | Suitability for `60 x 40` boxes | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Literal `40 cm` | Too tight in real life | Works only on paper with zero tolerance |
| `41-42 cm finished` | Good | Recommended for standard shelves |
| `60 cm` | More than enough | Reserve for tiles and bulky awkward items, not all boxes |

### Other box groups

Because exact measurements were not supplied for the IKEA Samla, Dunelm, and colour-coded boxes, the practical design assumption is:

- any box with a footprint at or below `60 x 40 cm` can live on the back shelves
- boxes deeper than `40 cm` should move either to the right deep bay upper shelf or be nested / consolidated

Before purchase, site-check the widest “Dunelm medium” and “green/pink” boxes against a target internal shelf depth of at least `41 cm`.

## 13. Construction Approach

### Recommended structural strategy

Use a **hybrid freestanding + wall-tied frame**, not wall-only shelving.

- Main vertical load goes to the floor through front legs / end frames.
- Back ledgers and side battens only stop racking and sway.
- Heavy shelves should sit on timber bearers fixed into the frame, not only into shed cladding.

### Practical framing method

1. Build the back wall as a ladder-frame shelving spine with front posts and back ledgers tied into the shed studs where available.
2. Build the right deep bay as a separate bench module tied into the back wall at the rear corner.
3. Build the left utility bench as a separate bench module tied into the back wall at the other rear corner.
4. Use diagonal restraint, back panels, or selected plywood gussets to stop sway.

### Suggested timber sizes

- `38 x 63 mm` CLS or planed softwood for most uprights, rails, and shelf bearers
- `38 x 89 mm` for any front beam that spans the full width of the right `60 cm` deep bench without a centre leg
- `18 mm` plywood for worktops and main shelves
- `12 mm` plywood or OSB for backs, dividers, and anti-racking panels

### Strength without overengineering

- Put a leg at each main bay division
- Keep long shelf spans around `70-75 cm` where possible
- Add a front nosing or batten to long plywood shelves if heavy boxes will live there
- Use wall fixings only as stabilizers into structural studs, not as the primary support for heavy loads

## 14. Materials Recommendation

| Material | Pros | Cons | Best use in this design |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Softwood plywood | Strong, dimensionally stable, cleaner edges, better screw holding | More expensive than OSB | Main shelves, bench tops, visible front edges |
| OSB3 | Cost-effective, structurally decent, good for hidden panels | Rougher finish, less pleasant for visible worktops, edges need care | Backing panels, hidden decking, non-visible dividers |
| MDF | Smooth and flat | Heavy, poor in damp conditions, weak edge screw holding | Not recommended except for very dry painted interiors |
| Melamine-faced board | Easy-clean surface | Heavy, awkward in damp sheds, chip-prone | Not recommended for the structure |

### Recommended combination

- **Best overall:** `18 mm` softwood plywood for shelves and both bench tops
- **Best budget compromise:** `18 mm` ply for shelf surfaces and bench tops, `12 mm` OSB3 for backs and non-visible panels
- **Avoid as primary structure:** MDF and chipboard in a garden shed

### Timber recommendation

- `38 x 63 mm` for most framing
- `38 x 89 mm` only where you want a stiffer front rail across the deep bay or a longer clear span
- `19 x 38 mm` battens are useful for cleats, front lips, and tool strips

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. Empty the shed and verify the actual internal wall-to-wall and floor-to-roof dimensions.
2. Find the main structural studs / frame members in the shed walls.
3. Snap or mark the finished front edge lines of the back wall shelves, right deep bay, and left bench on the floor.
4. Build and level the back wall frame first.
5. Fix the back ledgers into studs where available, but keep the weight on the floor through front posts.
6. Install the back shelves and confirm box fit before building the side units.
7. Build the right deep bay second and tie it into the back wall.
8. Install the low tile deck in the right deep bay.
9. Build the left utility bench third and tie it into the back wall.
10. Add the upper shelves, tool board, hooks, and ladder brackets.
11. Load the heaviest items first and watch for shelf deflection before filling upper levels.
12. Only after use-testing should you add optional front lips, bins, or door-side accessories.

It is the strongest option because it handles the real storage mix rather than pretending everything is the same shape:

- tiles get a proper low deep bay
- boxes get repeated `40 cm` shelf modules that actually fit
- ladders get secure dedicated storage
- bulky items are tucked under benches rather than dumped in the middle
- the centre of the shed still feels like space you can stand and work in

Practical caveats:

- Measure actual internal width and wall straightness before cutting the full `298 cm` shelf run.
- Build standard shelves to a **finished usable depth of `41-42 cm`**, not a literal hard `40 cm`.
- If the shed floor is lightweight, spread point loads under the tile bay and heavy-tool zones with a continuous plinth rather than isolated feet.
