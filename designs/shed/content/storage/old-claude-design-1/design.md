## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

### Wall allocation
- **Back wall (298 cm):** Full-length, floor-to-near-ceiling 40 cm deep shelving. The widest and tallest usable wall. Carries the bulk of box storage and lighter shelved items. Takes advantage of the full apex height (2.179 m) in the centre run.
- **Right short wall (175 cm):** A 60 cm deep zone for the rear 1.2 m — incorporating a workbench at 85 cm height with tile/bulky under-bench storage and shelves above. The front 55 cm stays shallow or open to preserve window light.
- **Left short wall (175 cm):** 40 cm deep shelving for the rear 1.2 m, plus a high-level horizontal ladder bracket system for the non-foldable ladder. Garden tools and foldable ladder at the front 55 cm section.

### Why mixed depth
The 60×120 cm tiles can only be stored efficiently in a 60 cm deep bay. Combining this with a workbench on the right wall achieves two goals with one structure — the deep bay provides tile storage and under-bench bulk storage, while the bench top gives a working surface. Standard 40 cm depth elsewhere keeps the central floor area as open as possible.

### Central floor space preserved
- Back wall shelves protrude 40 cm: leaves 135 cm clear depth.
- Right deep bay protrudes 60 cm from right wall.
- Left shelves protrude 40 cm from left wall.
- Central floor width in the back-shelf zone: 298 − 40 − 60 = **198 cm**.
- Front 55 cm zone (no side shelves): full **298 cm** width.
- Effective clear working/movement area: approximately **198 cm × 80 cm** in the primary zone, expanding to 298 cm wide toward the door.

### Workbench included
Yes. Integrated into the right wall deep bay. Bench top at 85 cm height, 120 cm wide × 60 cm deep. Tiles and bulk items stored below; shelves for tools and paint above.

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A — Back-Wall Dominant
Full-height back wall shelving at 40 cm deep runs the full 298 cm. Both short walls get 40 cm deep shelving only, with the tile bay handled by a low-level 60 cm deep section at one corner of the back/side junction. No dedicated workbench.

**Strengths:** Maximises box storage; simpler build; back wall does all the heavy lifting.  
**Weaknesses:** No good workbench; tile bay is awkward at a corner; no distinct zones; can feel like a shelving corridor.

### Concept B — Deep Side Bay + Workbench (Recommended)
Right short wall: 60 cm deep zone for 1.2 m from back, incorporating workbench + tile storage + upper shelves. Back wall: 40 cm deep full-length shelving. Left wall: 40 cm shelving + ladder storage.

**Strengths:** Tiles stored perfectly; workbench adds function; clear zones; visually ordered; central floor remains wide and open.  
**Weaknesses:** Slightly more complex build; loses ~20 cm of width versus Concept A in the deep bay zone.

### Concept C — Wrap-Around Maximiser
Both short walls and back wall all have 40 cm deep shelving for their full usable lengths. No workbench. Wall-hung tool panels wherever possible. Maximises number of shelf faces.

**Strengths:** Most pure shelving capacity; simple and repeatable.  
**Weaknesses:** No workbench; no 60 cm tile zone; feels less purposeful; tiles would need floor space.

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Concept B: Deep Side Bay + Workbench** is the recommended design.

**Why:** The 60 cm × 120 cm tiles are a specific dimensional constraint that this design solves elegantly — they fit perfectly flat under the workbench (bench top is exactly 120 cm wide × 60 cm deep). The workbench adds real utility for a shed that is described as needing "some working ability." The zoned approach (back wall for boxes, right bay for bulky/occasional-use items, left wall for tools and ladders) is intuitive and easy to maintain. The 198 cm clear floor width is adequate for two people working or wheeling items in and out.

## 11. Box-Fit Calculation

### 60 × 40 × 26 cm boxes (9 total)

- **Shelf depth:** 40 cm — box fits with 40 cm depth (exact fit). ✓
- **Box footprint along wall width:** 60 cm per box.
- **Per back wall shelf run (298 cm):** 298 ÷ 60 = 4.97 → **4 boxes per run** (leaving ~58 cm gap at end).
- **Shelf height needed:** 26 cm box + 4 cm clearance = **30 cm minimum shelf spacing**.
- **Shelf spacings used:** 35 cm (shelf 2 to 3) and 40 cm (shelf 3 to 4) — both adequate.
- **Positions available across back wall shelves 2 and 3:** 4 + 4 = **8 positions on back wall**.
- **Left wall shelf 3 provides:** 120 cm ÷ 60 = **2 positions** (but 1 slot used for other items).
- **Total positions for 9 boxes:** comfortably accommodated across back wall shelves 2–3 and left wall shelf 3.

- Fits a 40 cm deep shelf (39 cm depth). ✓
- 56 cm along wall, ~30 cm height clearance needed.
- 2 on Right shelf A, 1 on Left shelf 2.

## 13. Construction Approach

### Overall structure strategy: Hybrid (partially freestanding, partially wall-tied)

Given thin shed cladding walls, the storage should **not rely on wall cladding alone** to carry loaded shelves. Instead:

### Back wall shelving unit
- Build as a **freestanding floor-to-near-ceiling frame** standing in front of the back wall.
- Construct two vertical ladder frames from 75 × 50 mm PAR timber (or 44 × 94 mm CLS), spaced to suit shelf runs (~100 cm bays).
- Shelf boards: 18 mm plywood or 18 mm OSB, dadoed or directly resting on horizontal rails (44 × 44 mm timber batten).
- Back-tie to the wall via 2 × screws per upright into the shed's horizontal cladding rails or back sill plate — to prevent forward tipping, not to carry the load.
- Depth controlled by the depth of the horizontal rails (40 cm).

### Right wall deep bay + workbench
- Build as a **freestanding bench frame** from 75 × 50 mm PAR timber.
- Four legs (or two front legs + rear wall batten), cross-braced at floor and mid-height.
- Bench top: two sheets of 18 mm plywood glued and screwed = 36 mm total, or a single 25 mm sheet.
- Shelf standards above bench: use 75 × 50 mm uprights screwed to the bench frame below and tied back to the wall with a top batten.
- Under-bench tile platform: simple 40 × 40 mm batten frame with ply top, raised on feet at ~10 cm.

### Left wall shelving
- **Freestanding shelf unit** (same construction as back wall) for the 1.2 m × 40 cm section.
- **Ladder brackets:** Two sets of heavy-duty 200 mm × 40 mm wall-mounted steel angle brackets at 1.65 m and 1.75 m height. Fix via through-bolts to the shed's wall framing members (locate studs/rails first). These carry the horizontal ladder so loads are transferred into the shed frame, not the cladding.

### Tool hooks (left front zone + above bench)
- 18 mm ply panel screwed to the left wall front section framing.
- Standard pegboard hooks or simple bent-steel tool hooks.

### Fixings
- Structural joints: 75 × 5 mm wood screws (torx head), minimum 2 per joint.
- Shelf boards to rails: 50 × 5 mm wood screws.
- Wall ties (back tie only): 80 × 5 mm screws into wall rails/cladding ribs.
- Ladder brackets: M8 × 60 mm coach bolts with plate washers, into located wall studs.

## 14. Materials Recommendation

### Sheet materials

| Material | Pros | Cons | Best use in this design |
|----------|------|------|------------------------|
| 18 mm exterior plywood (BB/CC) | Strong, stable, holds screws, flat, moisture-resistant | More expensive than OSB | Bench top (one or two layers), shelf boards in prime zones |
| 18 mm OSB3 | Cheaper, adequate strength, handles shed humidity if sealed | Less attractive, screws on edges can loosen over time | Back wall shelf boards, side wall shelving where appearance matters less |
| 12 mm plywood | Lighter, quicker | Less stiff over long spans | Back shelf boards only in short bays (under 80 cm span) |

**Recommendation:** Use 18 mm plywood for the bench top and left wall shelving (higher use, more load). Use 18 mm OSB3 for the back wall shelving (long spans, but budget-conscious). Seal OSB edges and surfaces with a single coat of exterior wood primer to resist moisture in a shed environment.

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. **Measure and mark.** Verify all shed internal dimensions. Mark stud/rail positions on walls with a pencil. Confirm door swing clearance (door opens outward — no internal clearance issue).
2. **Build back wall shelving frame.** Construct the two ladder frames flat on the shed floor, then stand and tie back to wall. Fit horizontal shelf rails, level and brace.
3. **Fit back wall shelf boards.** Cut sheets to width. Fit from bottom shelf upward. Do not permanently fix top shelf until ladder clearance is confirmed.
4. **Build right wall bench frame.** Construct bench frame (legs + cross-members). Position 120 cm from back wall. Level. Secure top tie-batten to wall.
5. **Fit bench top.** Cut and screw 18 mm ply bench top. Sand edges if needed.
6. **Fit above-bench shelves.** Fit uprights above the bench frame. Fit horizontal shelf rails. Fit shelf boards.
7. **Build tile platform.** Construct simple 40 × 40 mm batten frame with ply top, raised at 10 cm. Slide into under-bench space.
8. **Build left wall shelving unit.** Construct and stand the left wall shelf unit (1.2 m long). Back-tie to wall.
9. **Fit left wall shelf boards.** Fit all shelf levels on left wall unit.
10. **Install ladder brackets.** Locate wall studs/rails. Drill and fit coach-bolted ladder brackets at 1.65 m height, at two positions 120 cm apart on left wall.
11. **Fit tool panel and hooks.** Screw plywood panel to left front wall framing. Fit tool hooks, extension cord hook, fertiliser hooks.
12. **Fit foldable ladder holder.** Simple pair of L-brackets or strap loops at left front section to hold folded ladder vertical.
13. **Seal OSB.** Apply one coat of exterior primer to all OSB surfaces and edges.
14. **Load and organise.** Bring in items in order: floor-level heavy items first, then shelved items, then boxes.

## 19. Final Recommendation

**Concept B — Deep Side Bay + Workbench** is the optimal solution for this shed.

The design achieves:
- **All 26 storage boxes** accommodated on shelves with appropriate spacing and clearance.
- **Tiles** stored perfectly flat in a purpose-designed 60 × 120 cm bay under the workbench.
- **BBQ and Kärcher** tucked away in the under-bench bay, accessible without disrupting daily use.
- **Two ladders** stored safely — one horizontal at high level, one vertical near the door.
- **Garden tools** on wall hooks at the door, the most-used items easiest to reach.
- **Central floor space** of approximately 198 cm wide × 135 cm deep preserved.
- A **workbench** that makes the shed genuinely useful, not just a box store.

**Key caveats:**
- Measure internally on-site before cutting. Cladding thickness, internal rebates, and floor levelness can affect dimensions by 2–5 cm.
- The bench top depth is exactly 60 cm to match tile dimensions — verify this before construction.
- Seal all OSB surfaces before loading the shed; timber shed environments can be damp.
- Ladder brackets must be into structural wall members, not cladding only.
- The design leaves the front 55 cm of both side walls relatively open — resist the temptation to add tall shelving here, as it would reduce light and make the shed feel cramped.
