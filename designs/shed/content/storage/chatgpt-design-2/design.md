## Design Brief Summary

This proposal keeps the shed shell unchanged and designs only the internal storage fit-out for a `298 cm x 175 cm` apex-roof shed with a central outward-opening double door on the front wall and window zones of about `80 cm` on each side of the door. The front wall stays a non-storage wall. Storage is concentrated on the three usable walls only: the `298 cm` back wall plus the two `175 cm` short walls.

The recommended layout uses:

- a full-width `40-42 cm` deep storage spine on the back wall
- one `60 cm` deep left-side tile and bulky-storage dock in the rear `120 cm`
- one `40-42 cm` deep right-side workbench and tool-access wall in the rear `110 cm`
- shallow front-side hanging and parking zones so the entry remains bright, open, and usable

Assumptions used in this design:

- Dimensions are treated as practical internal working dimensions. Check actual internal cladding-to-cladding sizes before cutting.
- The stated front wall split of `80 cm + 138 cm + 80 cm` is used in the plan graphics, with the `138 cm` central opening treated as the approximate double-door width.
- Exact dimensions were only supplied for the `60 x 40 x 26 cm` boxes. Other box groups are treated as mixed smaller formats unless site-measured otherwise.
- Roof geometry is interpreted as a gable on the front and back walls, with the highest point on the back wall centre at `217.9 cm` and the short walls running under `190 cm` eaves.

## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

This layout treats the three usable walls as three different storage jobs, because the shed is too small for one shelving pattern to solve everything cleanly.

- The **back wall** becomes the main `40-42 cm` deep box spine. It takes the repeated storage-box modules, heavy tools low, paints in trays, and the high horizontal rack for the `2.0 m` ladder.
- The **left short wall** becomes the single deep dock: `120 cm` long x `60 cm` deep, used for the tile deck at floor level plus a deep staging shelf above it. This keeps the densest and heaviest storage on the less-active side of the shed.
- The **right short wall** becomes the daily-use workbench and tool-access wall: `110 cm` long x `42 cm` deep bench with open storage below, plus shallow wall-hung storage above and a front-right cutter / BBQ pocket.

Why this mixed-depth approach works in this shed:

- A full `60 cm` depth around all three usable walls would feel too corridor-like for a `175 cm` deep shed.
- A pure `40 cm` design would preserve space but would not deal properly with `60 x 120 cm` tiles.
- Putting the `60 cm` depth on only one rear short-wall bay solves the tile problem while preserving a broad standing zone.
- Putting the active workbench on the **right short wall**, which is the side nearer the house, makes the most-used tools easier to grab without digging into the left-side deep storage dock.

Clear-space result:

- Standing depth in front of the back wall storage remains about `133 cm` (`175 - 42`).
- Clear rear working width between the left deep dock and the right workbench remains about `196 cm` (`298 - 60 - 42`).
- The front entry stays visually open because the front `55-65 cm` of both short walls is kept shallow or open rather than filled with tall cabinets.

This keeps the central floor usable for standing, turning, and short bench tasks, while still absorbing awkward items that usually end up dumped on the floor.

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A: Back-Wall Box Spine

**Description**  
Use the back wall for most of the storage and keep both short walls mostly open except for hooks or low bins.

**Strengths**

- Best clear central floor area
- Simplest carpentry
- Very efficient for repeated box storage

**Weaknesses**

- Weak solution for `60 x 120 cm` tiles
- Bulky items still compete for floor space
- No convincing workbench / tool wall zone

### Concept B: Left Tile Dock + Back Spine + Right Tool Bench

**Description**  
Use the back wall for standard `40-42 cm` shelving, the rear left short wall for the single `60 cm` deep tile dock, and the rear right short wall for a shallower daily-use workbench with the main hanging-tool zone.

**Strengths**

- Solves the tile requirement cleanly without deepening the whole shed
- Gives the shed a real working wall for frequent-use tools
- Preserves a broad, credible central standing zone

**Weaknesses**

- Slightly more carpentry than a single shelving wall
- Raw cubic shelf volume is lower than a dense rear U-shape

### Concept C: Rear U-Wrap Capacity Layout

**Description**  
Use the back wall and the rear `120 cm` of both short walls for semi-tall storage, with shallow shelves continuing further forward.

**Strengths**

- Maximum overall storage volume
- Easy to segregate item categories by wall
- Can swallow many boxes without re-packing

**Weaknesses**

- Most likely to feel like a narrow corridor
- More visual bulk near the side windows
- Less comfortable for actual work inside the shed

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Recommended concept: Concept B, “Back Box Spine + Left Tile Dock + Right Tool Bench.”**

It is the best option because it solves the real awkward items rather than pretending everything is a box:

- the tiles get a true `60 cm` deep low bay
- the back wall still behaves like a repeatable `40-42 cm` box-storage system
- the right wall becomes a real workbench and tool-access wall for day-to-day use
- the house-side wall carries the frequent-use zone, which is the more convenient side to approach
- tall and deep short-wall storage stays inside the rear `120 cm`, which respects the window-light constraint

- **Left front shallow strip:** `55 cm` long, about `18-20 cm` deep, hanging storage only

## 11. Box-Fit Calculation

### Known governing box size

- Known box footprint: `60 x 40 x 26 cm`
- Shelf depth target for the nominal `40 cm` shelves should be **about `42 cm` finished depth**, not a paper-thin literal `40 cm`

That allows:

- the `40 cm` box depth to sit fully on the shelf
- a modest back stop or front lip
- tolerance for handles, bowing, and wall irregularity

### Back-wall box capacity

Back wall width is `298 cm`. This design uses three practical shelf modules of:

- `80 cm` left bay
- `138 cm` centre bay
- `80 cm` right bay

Box fit per shelf run:

- B1 (`80 cm`): `1` box
- B2 (`138 cm`): `2` boxes
- B3 (`80 cm`): `1` box

So one full-width shelf run stores **`4` of the `60 x 40` boxes**.

If three shelf levels are allocated to those larger boxes:

- `4 boxes x 3 levels = 12 box positions`

That comfortably covers the known `9` large `60 x 40 x 26` boxes and leaves `3` spare positions for mixed containers or future additions.

### Shelf spacing

For `26 cm` high boxes:

- minimum usable clear opening: about `29 cm`
- preferred clear opening: about `31-33 cm`

Recommended back-wall shelf clear heights:

## 13. Construction Approach

### Recommended structural strategy

Use a **hybrid freestanding + wall-tied frame**, not wall-only shelving.

- Main vertical load goes to the floor through front legs / end frames.
- Back ledgers and side battens only stabilize the units and reduce sway.
- Heavy shelves and the tile dock sit on timber frames and plinths, not on shed cladding.

### Practical framing method

1. Build the back wall as a ladder-frame shelving spine with front posts and back ledgers tied into the shed studs where available.
2. Build the left deep dock as a separate bench-height carcass tied into the back wall at the rear left corner.
3. Build the right workbench as a separate bench module tied into the back wall at the rear right corner.
4. Use selected plywood backs, corner gussets, or diagonal braces to stop racking.

### Suggested timber sizes

- `38 x 63 mm` CLS or planed softwood for most uprights, rails, shelf bearers, and bench framing
- `38 x 89 mm` for any front rail spanning the full `120 cm` deep dock without a mid leg
- `18 mm` plywood for main shelves, bench tops, and the tile dock top
- `12 mm` plywood or OSB3 for backs, dividers, gussets, and hidden anti-racking panels

### Strength without overengineering

- Put a leg at each main bay division
- Keep normal shelf spans around `75-80 cm` where possible
- Add a front nosing or batten under long plywood shelves that will carry repeated heavy boxes
- Use wall screws into real frame members only as stabilizers, not as the primary support

## 14. Materials Recommendation

| Material | Pros | Cons | Best use in this design |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Softwood plywood | Strong, good screw holding, cleaner cut edges, nicer worktop feel | More expensive than OSB | Main shelves, bench tops, visible fronts |
| OSB3 | Cost-effective, structurally sound, fine for hidden panels | Rougher finish, less pleasant for worktops, edges need care | Back panels, hidden decks, non-visible dividers |
| Moisture-resistant MDF | Smooth and paintable | Heavy, weaker edge fixing, poor long-term shed material if damp | Not recommended for the structure |
| Melamine-faced chipboard | Easy wipe-clean finish | Heavy, chip-prone, poor in damp shed conditions | Not recommended for primary storage structure |

### Recommended combination

- **Best overall:** `18 mm` softwood plywood for shelves, both tops, and the tile dock deck surface
- **Best budget compromise:** `18 mm` ply for visible and load-bearing surfaces, `12 mm` OSB3 for backs and hidden stiffeners
- **Avoid as primary structure:** MDF and chipboard in an outdoor shed environment

### Timber recommendation

- `38 x 63 mm` for most framing
- `38 x 89 mm` only where a stiffer deep-bay front rail is useful
- `19 x 38 mm` battens for shelf lips, tool-board cleats, and shallow hanging rails

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. Empty the shed and verify the actual internal wall-to-wall and floor-to-roof dimensions.
2. Find the main structural studs / frame members in the shed walls.
3. Mark the finished front edge lines of the back shelving, left deep dock, and right workbench on the floor.
4. Build and level the back wall frame first.
5. Fix back ledgers into studs where available, but keep the real load on the floor through front posts.
6. Install the back shelves and confirm box fit before locking in the side units.
7. Build the left deep dock second and tie it into the back wall.
8. Install the low tile deck and check that the deck spreads load well across the shed floor.
9. Build the right workbench third and tie it into the back wall.
10. Add the right-side tool board, shallow shelves, cutter rail, and left-side ladder strip.
11. Fit ladder brackets and hook hardware only after the main structure is square and tested.
12. Load the heaviest items first and watch for any shelf deflection before filling upper levels.

## 19. Final Recommendation

The best solution for this shed is the **back-wall box spine plus one `60 cm` deep left tile dock plus one `42 cm` deep right workbench and tool wall**.

It is the strongest option because it balances the shed like a real working space:

- dense tile and bulky storage is pushed to one rear-side dock
- the back wall still works efficiently for repeated boxes
- the house-side wall becomes the frequent-use working wall
- the awkward items all have named homes
- the centre of the shed still feels like a place you can stand, turn, and do short tasks

Practical caveats:

- Measure the actual internal width, wall straightness, and window sill positions before final cutting.
- Build standard shelves to a **finished usable depth of `42 cm`**, not a literal hard `40 cm`.
- If the shed floor is lightweight, spread the deep-dock tile load over a full plinth or continuous base rather than isolated feet.
