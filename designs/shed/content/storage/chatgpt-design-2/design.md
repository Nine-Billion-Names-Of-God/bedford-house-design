## Design Brief Summary

This proposal keeps the shed shell unchanged and designs only the internal fit-out for a `298 cm x 175 cm` shed with:

- a front wall treated as non-storage
- central outward-opening double doors of about `138 cm`
- a window zone of about `80 cm` on each side of the doors
- an apex roof with a stated high point of `217.9 cm` and a lower wall/eaves height of `190 cm`

Storage is built only on the three usable walls:

- the `298 cm` back wall
- the `175 cm` left short wall
- the `175 cm` right short wall

The selected layout uses:

- one full back-wall shelving spine at a nominal `42 cm` depth
- one rear-left deep dock at `120 cm x 60 cm`, sized deliberately for the `60 x 120 cm` tiles
- one rear-right `115 cm x 42 cm` workbench with open under-bench storage and a timber-backed tool wall
- shallow or open front-side strips so the shed keeps light, door usability, and a credible central standing area

Assumptions used in this design:

- The quoted dimensions are treated as practical inside working dimensions for planning. Site-check actual inside-to-inside sizes before cutting sheet goods.
- The roof is interpreted as giving the greatest usable height at the centre of the back wall and lower eaves on the short walls. If the actual ridge direction proves different on site, keep the same floor plan but move the highest shelf / ladder rack to the true high wall.
- Only the `60 x 40 x 26 cm` box group has exact dimensions. All other named box groups are treated as approximate medium or small consumer storage boxes and should be measured before final shelf spacing is fixed.
- The nominal “`40 cm`” storage depth is built here as about `42 cm` finished depth so `40 cm` deep boxes can be handled without scraping the wall or shelf lip.

| Constraint | Stated condition | Practical effect on the design |

| Front wall | Approx. `80 + 138 + 80 cm` window-door-window split | Front wall is treated as non-storage |
| Usable walls | Back wall plus two short walls | Storage must work as a three-wall system |
| Roof | Apex roof, `217.9 cm` high point, `190 cm` at side walls | Highest storage belongs where the roof geometry truly allows it; side-wall storage must stay honest to the lower eaves |
| Short-wall window rule | Tall storage on a short wall should stop at about `1.2 m` from the back wall | The front ends of both short walls must remain lower, shallower, or open |
| Mixed-depth rule | At least one `60 cm` deep zone, most other storage around `40 cm` | A single-depth scheme would not solve the brief properly |
| Tile requirement | Tiles are `60 x 120 cm` | Best solved by one low rear deep dock rather than by forcing every wall deeper |
| Item mix | Boxes, ladders, Kärcher, BBQ, toolbox, paints, tools, fertiliser, tiles | Need shelving, hanging storage, under-bench voids, and a few open bulky-item pockets |
| Access priorities | Hand tools, cutters, cord, fertiliser, and common garden tools are frequent-use | These need a dedicated hanging or bench-adjacent access zone, not random deep shelves |
| Construction preference | Timber framing plus plywood / OSB / similar | Custom timber units are appropriate; metal/plastic shelving is not |
| Shed wall strength | Thin shed walls | The units must be floor-bearing and only lightly tied back to the shed framing for anti-rack stability |

## 2. Overall Storage Strategy

This shed works best when each usable wall is given a distinct job.

- The **back wall** becomes the main repeatable storage spine. It carries the box runs, paint trays, spare materials, and a high ladder rack.
- The **left short wall** becomes the single deep dock. Its rear `120 cm x 60 cm` footprint matches the tile format directly, which means the heaviest awkward item is solved cleanly instead of being tolerated badly.
- The **right short wall** becomes the active daily-use side. It carries the workbench, the hanging-tool board, the extension cord, fertiliser, and open under-bench space for the Kärcher and toolbox.

Why the mixed-depth arrangement is right here:

- Making every wall `60 cm` deep would waste too much floor area in a `175 cm` deep shed.
- Making every wall about `40 cm` deep would handle boxes well but solve the tiles badly.
- One purposeful deep dock plus mostly `42 cm` storage keeps the central floor open while still meeting the hardest storage requirement honestly.

How central floor space is preserved:

- Clear depth in front of the back shelving remains about `133 cm` (`175 - 42`).
- Clear rear width between the `60 cm` left dock and the `42 cm` right bench remains about `196 cm` (`298 - 60 - 42`).
- The short-wall front zones stay lighter, so the front third of the shed does not feel pinched even after the awkward items are accounted for.

Workbench / under-bench decision:

- A workbench is included on the right wall because this shed needs a controlled “active” side, not just stacked storage.
- The under-bench void is used for bulky medium-priority items.
- The tool board above the bench is the correct place for secateurs, trowels, pruners, loppers, hedge shears, pruning saws, gloves, and the extension cord.

This arrangement gives the shed a clear logic:

- heavy and awkward low
- repeated boxes on the back spine
- daily-use tools visible on the right wall
- front wall and door zone kept clear

## 3. Layout Concept Options

### Concept A: Back-Wall Shelving Only

**Description**  
Use the `298 cm` back wall for nearly all box and shelf storage, with only very shallow hooks or clips on the short walls.

**Key strengths**

- Best central openness
- Simplest build
- Strongest repeated shelf efficiency for boxes

**Key weaknesses**

- Weak answer to the `60 x 120 cm` tile requirement
- Bulky items would drift onto the floor
- No convincing workbench-plus-tool-wall zone

### Concept B: Left Tile Dock + Back Spine + Right Workbench

**Description**  
Use the back wall as the main `42 cm` shelf spine, the rear-left short wall as a `60 cm` deep tile dock, and the rear-right short wall as a shallower workbench and tool wall.

**Key strengths**

- Solves the tile requirement properly
- Gives daily-use tools a deliberate home
- Preserves a strong open middle
- Supports occasional working as well as storage

**Key weaknesses**

- More carpentry than a single shelving run
- Slightly less raw cubic storage than a more aggressive U-wrap

### Concept C: Rear U-Wrap Maximum Capacity

**Description**  
Build shelving onto the back wall and both rear short-wall sections, using as much full-height storage as the shed will tolerate.

**Key strengths**

- Highest raw cubic storage
- Easy category separation by wall
- Strong “everything has a bay” feeling

**Key weaknesses**

- Highest corridor risk
- Heaviest visual mass near the windows
- Less comfortable for working and retrieval

## 5. Final Recommended Layout

**Selected concept: Concept B, “Left Tile Dock + Back Spine + Right Workbench.”**

This is the best solution because it solves the difficult parts of the brief directly instead of pretending everything is “just shelving”:

- the tiles get a true low `120 x 60 cm` bay that actually matches the tile format
- the back wall still functions as a repeatable box-storage field
- the most-used tools get a real access wall beside a real working surface
- the heaviest dense items stay low
- the short-wall front-light rule is respected because the tall / deep rear sections stop before the window zones

## 11. Box-Fit Calculation

### Known exact box size

Only one box group has exact dimensions in the brief:

- `60 x 40 x 26 cm` boxes: `9` units

### Planning allowance used

For realistic retrieval clearance, the design does **not** assume zero-gap storage. The working planning footprint used here is:

- width allowance per large box: about `62 cm`
- depth allowance per large box: about `41 cm`
- clear shelf height per large box: about `30-32 cm`

### How many fit on the back shelf run

Back shelf width is `298 cm`.

`298 / 62 = 4.8`, so the practical design number is:

- **4 large boxes per shelf run**

That breaks down naturally as:

- `1` box in the `80 cm` left functional zone
- `2` boxes in the `138 cm` centre functional zone
- `1` box in the `80 cm` right functional zone

### Capacity of the proposed back shelves

Three dedicated box levels on the back wall give:

- `4 boxes x 3 levels = 12 large-box positions`

So the design stores all `9` known large boxes with:

- `3` spare positions left for growth or easier spacing

### Is `42 cm` depth enough?

Yes, for the known `40 cm` deep boxes, a finished shelf depth of about `42 cm` is the right target. It is deep enough to carry the box without a dangerous overhang but not so deep that the shed loses unnecessary floor area.

Before cutting, check the actual clear depth of the finished shelf carcass. If the box lids flare beyond `40 cm`, hold the front lip back and keep the back batten slim so the true usable depth stays above about `41 cm`.

### Shelf spacing needed

For `26 cm` tall boxes, a practical clear opening is:

- minimum comfortable clear height: about `30 cm`
- better working clear height: about `31-32 cm`

That is why the back-wall shelf surfaces are pitched at roughly:

- `18 cm`
- `52 cm`
- `86 cm`
- `120 cm`
- `156 cm`

With `18 mm` sheet shelves, that gives enough hand clearance to lift boxes on and off without wasting too much vertical space.

### Other box groups

The other named box groups do not have confirmed dimensions in the brief. This design assumes most of them are still compatible with a `42 cm` deep shelf, but that must be checked before purchase or final cut.

Remaining named boxes:

- IKEA Samla medium: `3`
- small boxes: `4`
- IKEA green and pink boxes: `2`
- Dunelm medium: `6`
- Dunelm small: `2`

That is `17` additional mixed-format boxes.

Linear frontage available for these mixed boxes is roughly:

- back top shelf: `298 cm`
- back mixed shelf: `298 cm`
- left rear upper shelf: `90 cm`
- right shallow upper shelf: `115 cm`

Total upper / mixed frontage: about `801 cm`

At a rough planning frontage of `45 cm` per mixed box, that is room for about:

- `801 / 45 = 17.8`

So the remaining `17` mixed boxes are credible within the scheme, provided the deepest actual box types do not exceed the intended shelf depth.

## 12. Workbench / Under-Bench Storage Proposal

The workbench is included because this shed is not only a store; it also benefits from a controlled standing-height working edge.

- **Location:** right short wall, rear section
- **Bench run:** `115 cm`
- **Bench depth:** `42 cm`
- **Bench height:** `91 cm`

What goes underneath:

- Kärcher in the rear bay
- toolbox in the front bay
- a narrow holster or crate for dense heavy hand tools

What goes above:

- timber-backed tool board for hand tools and cutters
- one shallow shelf at about `150 cm`
- small hook points for gloves, cord, and accessories

Is this the best place for the tile bay?

- No. The tiles belong on the opposite wall.

Reason:

- The tiles are dense, flat, and infrequent-use.
- The exact `120 x 60 cm` left dock solves them elegantly.
- Putting tiles below the bench would waste the best daily-use wall and would also fight with the Kärcher and toolbox.

The workbench side should stay active and easy to reach. The tile side should stay dense and quiet.

## 13. Construction Approach

### Recommended structural approach

Use a **hybrid freestanding / lightly wall-tied** build:

- The heavy storage units should stand on the floor through their own legs and frames.
- The shed wall should only stabilize the units, square them, and stop sway.
- Do **not** rely on thin shed cladding to carry tile loads, toolbox loads, or repeated heavy box shelves.

### Practical framing strategy

- Build the back shelving as a floor-bearing timber frame with front legs, rear uprights, and repeated bays of about `70-75 cm`.
- Build the left tile dock as a separate heavy low frame, then screw it into the back shelving return so the corner behaves as one assembly.
- Build the right bench as another floor-bearing frame, then tie it into the back shelving and the nearest wall battens.

### Sensible reinforcement

- Find the real shed studs / rails first.
- Fix `38 x 50 mm` or similar battens to those studs where useful.
- Screw the storage frames to those battens only after the frames are standing level on the floor.
- Use sheet back panels or triangular corner blocking at the back corners to stop racking.

### Keeping it strong without overengineering it

- Use proper vertical legs at practical spans instead of giant brackets.
- Use `18 mm` sheet material for the structural shelf decks.
- Add front nosings or front rails where longer shelves might sag.
- Put the heaviest items on the low levels instead of trying to make the upper shelves “bombproof”.

## 14. Materials Recommendation

### Plywood

**Pros**

- Better screw holding at edges
- Cleaner cut edges and better finish
- Stiffer and more reliable for shelf decks and bench tops
- Better choice in a damp shed if you can afford exterior / WBP grade

**Cons**

- Higher cost
- Can feel wasteful if used for every hidden panel

**Best use in this design**

- back shelf decks
- workbench top
- tile-dock top and deck
- shelf end panels and any exposed faces

### OSB3

**Pros**

- Lower cost
- Strong enough for hidden backs, webs, and non-finish parts
- Readily available in shed-friendly sizes

**Cons**

- Rougher edges
- Less pleasant as an exposed bench or shelf surface
- Not as nice for small hooks and repeated accessory screw changes

**Best use in this design**

- hidden back panels
- tool-board backing if appearance is not critical
- stiffening webs, gussets, and internal dividers

### Other sensible sheet materials

Melamine-faced chipboard is not recommended for the main structure here. It hates damp, chips easily, and is not the right material for a shed with mixed heavy storage and DIY use.

### Recommended timber sizes

- **Main legs and primary rails:** `45 x 70 mm` CLS or similar finished section
- **Secondary rails / wall battens / shelf cleats:** `38 x 50 mm`
- **Optional front shelf nosings / lips:** `18 x 44 mm` or similar
- **Main shelves, bench top, tile deck:** `18 mm` plywood preferred
- **Backs / tool board / stiffeners:** `12 mm` plywood or `12 mm` OSB3

## 16. Practical Build Sequence

1. Empty the shed and measure the true internal width, depth, floor level, and stud positions.
2. Mark the non-storage front wall and the rear `120 cm` / `115 cm` short-wall limits so the window-light rule does not drift during building.
3. Build and level the back shelving frame first because it sets the geometry for everything else.
4. Deck the back shelves and confirm that a `60 x 40` box fits with comfortable clearance before fixing every level permanently.
5. Build the left deep tile dock and tie it into the back shelving return.
6. Build the right bench frame, then fix on the bench top and under-bench divider / holster.
7. Add wall battens, the tool-board panel, shallow upper shelves, and the ladder clip / rack hardware.
8. Load the heaviest items first: tiles, toolbox, Kärcher, paint trays.
9. Load the known large boxes on the back wall next, then fine-tune one adjustable upper shelf for the mixed box groups.
10. Finish by installing hooks, labels, and front-edge lips where loose items need retention.

## 17. Safety / Access / Usability Checklist

- Heavy items are kept low: tiles, toolbox, Kärcher, paint trays, and dense hand tools are all at floor to bench height.
- Tiles are stored flat and low on a dedicated deck, not upright against thin cladding.
- The double-door swing is not obstructed because the front wall remains a non-storage wall.
- The windows stay visually open because tall / deep short-wall storage is confined to the rear section.
- The short-wall rear units stop before the front third, so the shed does not read like a tunnel.
- The central standing area remains usable at about `196 cm` rear clear width and `133 cm` clear depth in front of the back shelving.
- The `2.0 m` ladder is secured on a deliberate rack rather than leaning loose.
- The folding ladder is clipped on a shallow front-side strip rather than dumped on the floor.
- Frequently used tools remain visible and reachable on the right-side tool board.
- The BBQ has an intentional floor pocket, so it does not drift into the centre of the shed.
- Heavy tools are not stored overhead where they could fall.

## 19. Final Recommendation

The best answer for this shed is a **back shelf spine + left tile dock + right workbench tool wall**.

It is the strongest option because it uses the full `298 cm` back wall for efficient repeated shelf storage, gives the `60 x 120 cm` tiles a genuinely correct low bay, and reserves the best day-to-day wall for a practical bench and visible tool-access zone. The front wall stays clear, the windows keep light, and the central floor remains believable once the awkward items are shown honestly.

Practical caveats:

- Check the actual ridge direction and inside dimensions before fixing the highest shelf and ladder rack.
- Measure the non-specified box groups before finalizing every shelf position.
- If the floor is uneven, spend time shimming and levelling the base frames first; the whole fit-out will work better afterward.
