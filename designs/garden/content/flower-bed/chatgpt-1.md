## 1. Summary of constraints

My understanding is:

- You have **two very narrow side beds** flanking the patio, each about **2.1m long**.
- The **right bed** is about **0.3m wide** and is the main **kitchen-herb bed**.
- The **left bed** is even tighter at **0.2–0.3m wide**, so it needs a simpler, more restrained mix.
- Both beds are **east-facing** with **morning sun and afternoon shade**, on **London clay**, with only **20–30cm soil depth**.
- The **right-side tap near the house must stay easy to reach**, so the first stretch of that bed should stay **low and open**.
- The planting must be **herb-forward**, **compact**, **repetition-based**, and include **climbers on the fences**.

So this is really a **narrow, shallow, usable planting-strip problem**, not a normal border.

## 2. Overall planting strategy

Because east-facing gardens are cooler and shadier for much of the day, and many sun-lovers flower less in partial shade, I would bias the core palette toward **parsley, chives, chervil and heuchera-backed combinations**, then use **thyme and oregano only in the brightest, best-drained pockets**. RHS also notes that most herbs want **well-drained, moisture-retentive soil with organic matter**, while heucheras thrive in **light shade**, and chives, parsley and chervil all cope well in **part shade**. ([RHS][1])

For these beds I would use a very simple structure:

- **One main planting line** of repeated clumps, not mixed randomly.
- **One climber per bed**, trained flat to the fence.
- **Low edging only where width allows**, especially creeping thyme, violets or restrained fleabane.
- **No bulky shrubs**.
- **Right bed first 40–50cm kept low** for tap access.
- Mediterranean herbs are used, but only sparingly, because thyme, oregano and sage all still want the **sharpest drainage**. ([RHS][2])

A good mental model is: **long trough planting with a fence backdrop**.

# 3. Planting Scheme A — herb-forward

**Character:** most practical and kitchen-led.
**Best for:** daily picking, easiest harvesting, restrained green look.

**Repetition pattern**

- **Right bed:** Parsley → Chives → Thyme → Parsley → Chives
- **Left bed:** Chervil → Parsley → Chives → Chervil → Parsley

This scheme leans hardest into the herbs that handle part shade best, with thyme only used as a low edge/filler where drainage is best. ([RHS][3])

### Scheme A — Right bed (herb-focused, near kitchen)

| Plant name                                                                | Qty |                      Spacing | Mature height / spread   | Role          |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --: | ---------------------------: | ------------------------ | ------------- |
| Flat-leaf parsley (_Petroselinum crispum_ var. _neapolitanum_) ([RHS][4]) |   2 |                      25–30cm | 25–40cm H × 20–30cm S    | Herb          |
| Chives (_Allium schoenoprasum_) ([RHS][5])                                |   2 |                         25cm | 20–30cm H × 10–20cm S    | Herb          |
| Creeping/common thyme (_Thymus serpyllum_ / _T. vulgaris_) ([RHS][6])     |   2 |                         25cm | 5–30cm H × 20–40cm S     | Herb / edging |
| Clematis alpina ([RHS][7])                                                |   1 | 1 plant in outer half of bed | 1.5–2.5m H, trained flat | Climber       |

**Maintenance notes:**
Keep the first **40–50cm nearest the tap** to low thyme only, or even mostly open mulch. Cut parsley and chives regularly. Trim thyme after flowering to keep it tight.

**Maintenance notes:**
Harvest chervil young and often so it stays soft and airy rather than tall. Keep violets clipped after flowering if they start to creep onto the paving.

# 4. Planting Scheme B — balanced herb + flowers

**Character:** softer and prettier, but still very usable.
**Best for:** strongest all-round balance of kitchen use and appearance.

**Repetition pattern**

- **Right bed:** Oregano → Parsley → Chives → Oregano → Parsley
- **Left bed:** Chervil → Heuchera → Chives → Heuchera → Chervil

Golden oregano is useful here because RHS notes it can take **partial shade** and is good as an **edging herb**, while heucheras are compact, low-maintenance and strong in **light shade**. ([RHS][10])

### Scheme B — Right bed

| Plant name                                                                | Qty |                      Spacing | Mature height / spread     | Role          |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --: | ---------------------------: | -------------------------- | ------------- |
| Golden oregano (_Origanum vulgare_ ‘Aureum’) ([RHS][11])                  |   2 |                      25–30cm | 20–30cm H × 25–35cm S      | Herb          |
| Flat-leaf parsley (_Petroselinum crispum_ var. _neapolitanum_) ([RHS][4]) |   2 |                      25–30cm | 25–40cm H × 20–30cm S      | Herb          |
| Chives (_Allium schoenoprasum_) ([RHS][5])                                |   1 |                         25cm | 20–30cm H × 10–20cm S      | Herb          |
| Creeping thyme (_Thymus serpyllum_ / low thyme cultivar) ([RHS][12])      |   2 |                         25cm | 5–10cm H × 20–30cm S       | Herb / edging |
| Clematis ‘Frances Rivis’ ([RHS][13])                                      |   1 | 1 plant in outer half of bed | about 2.4m H, trained flat | Climber       |

**Maintenance notes:**
This is still very functional. Keep thyme and oregano in the driest pockets. Trim oregano lightly after flowering to stop it loosening too much.

This scheme looks fuller for longer. Divide or re-seat heucheras every few years if crowns lift, which RHS notes can happen because they are shallow-rooted, especially on heavier soils. ([RHS][16])

# 5. Planting Scheme C — more ornamental but still usable

**Character:** most decorative and designed-looking.
**Best for:** soft, pretty patio edges with usable herbs still built in.

**Repetition pattern**

- **Right bed:** Variegated sage → Chives → Golden oregano → Parsley → Chives
- **Left bed:** Heuchera → Parsley → Erigeron → Chives → Heuchera

This version pushes foliage contrast and longer ornamental performance. It is still usable, but slightly less purely culinary than A or B. Star jasmine gives the strongest evergreen vertical effect, but it is also the most demanding option here because RHS recommends a **warm, sheltered, well-drained** position and support wires or trellis. ([RHS][17])

# 6. Climbers summary (across schemes)

| Climber                                                      | Why it suits                                                            | Size           | Support requirement                                                       | Best use here                                                            |
| ------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Clematis alpina** ([RHS][7])                               | East-facing, partial-shade tolerant, manageable, good for small gardens | 1.5–2.5m       | Slim trellis or 2–3 horizontal wires; tie young stems in                  | **Best technical fit**                                                   |
| **Clematis ‘Frances Rivis’** ([RHS][22])                     | Similar fit to alpina, prettier blue spring flowers                     | about 2.4m     | Same as above; very light pruning only after flowering if needed          | **Best all-round choice**                                                |
| **Star jasmine** (_Trachelospermum jasminoides_) ([RHS][20]) | Evergreen, scented, strong vertical greenery                            | up to about 4m | Stronger trellis or tensioned wires fixed off the fence; regular tying-in | **Best ornamental choice**, but only in warmest, most sheltered sections |

**Practical note:** in beds only 20–30cm deep, I would use **just one climber per bed**, placed **away from the tap**, and train it **flat rather than bushy**.

# 8. Final recommended planting scheme

## **Recommended: Scheme B — balanced herb + flowers**

This is the one I would choose for your garden.

Why:

- It respects the **kitchen use** of the right bed.
- It fits the **east-facing, part-shade** conditions better than a heavily Mediterranean palette.
- It keeps the beds looking **green and soft**, not sparse.
- It stays **compact and manageable** in 20–30cm depth.
- It gives you enough flowers and foliage contrast to make the patio edges feel designed, not just edible. Parsley, chives, chervil and heucheras are all comfortable in lighter shade conditions, while golden oregano can still work as a bright herb accent in partial shade if drainage is kept good. ([RHS][3])
