# Front Garden Planting Design — Bedford Park, Chiswick

**Property:** Victorian terraced house, Bedford Park conservation area  
**Orientation:** West-facing  
**Garden dimensions:** 6.0 m frontage × 4.6 m depth (3.9 m at bay window)  
**Design area:** 4.0 m × 4.6 m (main garden, east of path)  
**Soil:** London clay  
**Style:** Structured but natural — Bedford Park character  

---

## 1. Summary of Key Constraints

| Constraint | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Orientation** | West-facing: morning shade, strong afternoon/evening sun |
| **Soil** | London clay — heavy, moisture-retentive, alkaline-trending |
| **Layout** | Fixed — layered boundary planting with open central shingle court |
| **Fixed elements** | Path, gate, front wall/fence, bike shed, house façade, bay window, water tap |
| **Bike access** | ≥1.0 m clearance in front of shed; shed doors must open fully |
| **Privacy** | Evergreen, layered, airy screening along front boundary — NOT a hedge |
| **Height limits** | Most planting ≤1.5 m; up to 2.0 m at front boundary only |
| **Bed depth** | 70–80 cm throughout |
| **Winter interest** | Garden must not feel empty in winter — evergreen structure essential |
| **Style** | Structured but natural; avoid formal geometry or loose cottage chaos |
| **Maintenance** | Moderate — appropriate for a working household |
| **Central court** | Shingle retained; bike circulation; optional focal plant if compact |
| **Water tap** | Right side of bay window — access must remain unobstructed |

---

## 2. Overall Planting Structure

The planting strategy follows the brief's layout: **peripheral beds enclosing an open central court**.

### Structural Layers (back to front within each bed)

```
HOUSE WALL   →  Climbers (2–3 m on trellis)
             →  Medium shrubs (0.8–1.2 m)
             →  Low perennials / edging (0.2–0.4 m)

CENTRAL COURT   →  Open shingle with optional focal plant

FRONT BOUNDARY  →  Tall evergreen screening (1.5–2.0 m) against fence
                →  Medium flowering shrubs (0.8–1.2 m)
                →  Low edging / spillover plants (0.2–0.4 m) over wall
```

### Design Principles Applied

- **Evergreen backbone:** At least 60% of structural planting is evergreen for winter presence.
- **Layered screening:** Front boundary uses 2–3 layers of varying height rather than a single hedge plane.
- **Seasonal relay:** Spring bulbs → early summer perennials → midsummer shrubs → autumn colour → winter structure/scent.
- **Scale discipline:** In a 4.0 m × 4.6 m space, every plant must earn its place. No plant wider than 1.2 m at maturity; most under 0.8 m.
- **Bedford Park character:** Hydrangeas, roses, lavender, star jasmine, box, and other plants seen in the conservation area's front gardens.

## 3. Zone-by-Zone Planting Recommendations

### Zone 1 — Left Hedge Strip (1.0 m × 4.6 m)

**Conditions:** Semi-shaded (hedge canopy above, neighbour structures either side). Drier at hedge base due to root competition. Narrow strip — maximum planting width ~60 cm after hedge trunk zone.

**Purpose:** Soften the hedge base, add seasonal ground-level interest, create a green corridor alongside the path.

**Approach:**
- Shade-tolerant, drought-tolerant ground-layer planting
- Evergreen base with seasonal bulbs
- Nothing taller than 40 cm to keep below hedge canopy
- Plants must tolerate root competition from the hedge

| Layer | Role | Height | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Evergreen ground cover | Year-round base | 15–30 cm | Vinca minor, Liriope muscari, Carex morrowii |
| Shade perennials | Spring/summer flower | 20–40 cm | Brunnera macrophylla, Tiarella, Epimedium |
| Bulbs | Seasonal highlights | 10–25 cm | Cyclamen hederifolium, Galanthus, Muscari |
| Ferns (optional) | Textural contrast | 30–40 cm | Polystichum setiferum, Dryopteris erythrosora |

---

### Zone 2 — House Wall Bed (~3.0–3.5 m usable length × 0.7–0.8 m depth)

**Conditions:** West-facing wall — warm microclimate, strong afternoon sun, reflected heat from brickwork. Sheltered from wind. Bed is interrupted by bay window projection (0.7 m). Water tap access required right of bay.

**Sub-zones:**
- **2a — Left of bay / near door:** ~1.0–1.5 m length. Ideal for a wall climber on trellis.
- **2b — Right of bay:** ~1.5–2.0 m length. Shrubs and perennials, but keep tap accessible.

**Approach:**
- Wall climber on trellis (ties into Bedford Park tradition of planted facades)
- Compact shrubs and perennials that tolerate reflected heat
- Fragrant plants near the front door
- Low edging to soften the bed/court transition

| Layer | Role | Height | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Climber (on trellis) | Vertical interest, soften wall | 2–3 m | Trachelospermum jasminoides, Clematis armandii |
| Medium shrub | Structure right of bay | 0.6–1.0 m | Sarcococca confusa, Daphne odora, compact Hydrangea |
| Perennials | Seasonal flower, fill gaps | 0.3–0.6 m | Heuchera, Astrantia, Geranium, Japanese anemone |
| Edging | Soften front of bed | 0.15–0.3 m | Erigeron karvinskianus, Alchemilla mollis |

**Tap access note:** Keep planting at least 40 cm clear of the water tap. Place lower plants (heuchera, erigeron) in that zone.

---

### Zone 3 — Front Boundary Bed (4.0 m wide × 0.7–0.8 m depth)

**Conditions:** Warmest bed — full afternoon/evening sun. Behind a ~1.5 m combined wall/fence. Most visible from the street. Shed sits immediately behind the right portion (from ~2.5 m to 3.5 m along the bed).

**Sub-zones:**
- **3a — Left section (0–2.5 m from path):** Full depth available, main privacy screening.
- **3b — Right section (2.5–4.0 m):** Shed behind restricts deep-root planting and maintenance access. Use shallower-rooted, lower-maintenance plants.

**Approach:**
- Tallest evergreen screening plants here (1.5–2.0 m) against the fence
- Layered with medium flowering shrubs in front
- Low plants to soften the top of the brick wall / spill slightly
- Must remain airy, not hedge-like — use plants with different textures and heights

| Layer | Role | Height | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tall evergreen screening | Privacy, fence-softening | 1.5–2.0 m | Viburnum tinus, Osmanthus × burkwoodii, Pittosporum tenuifolium |
| Medium flowering shrubs | Seasonal colour | 0.8–1.2 m | Hydrangea macrophylla, Rosa, Choisya ternata |
| Perennials | Front-of-bed colour | 0.3–0.6 m | Lavandula, Nepeta, Salvia, Geranium |
| Edging / spillover | Soften wall edge | 0.15–0.3 m | Erigeron karvinskianus, Aubrieta, Campanula portenschlagiana |

---

### Zone 4 — Central Shingle Court (~2.4–2.6 m wide)

**Conditions:** Open, full afternoon sun. Shingle surface retained. Must accommodate bike circulation (manoeuvring, shed door clearance).

**Approach:**
- Primarily open — no planting in circulation paths
- Optional single focal plant, positioned to avoid obstructing bike access
- Focal plant should be narrow, upright habit — no wider than 1.0–1.2 m
- Best position: centre-left of the court, offset from shed door swing zone

## 8. Central Focal Plant Options

The central shingle court can accommodate a single specimen plant that provides a vertical accent and focal interest. It must remain compact, not obstruct bike access, and provide multi-season interest.

**Placement:** Centre-left of the court, approximately 1.2–1.5 m from the front boundary and 1.0–1.2 m from the house wall bed. This keeps it clear of the shed door swing zone and bike manoeuvring space.

| # | Plant | Mature H × W | Habit | Key Interest | Maintenance | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Prunus incisa 'Kojo-no-mai'** (Fuji cherry) | 1.5–2.5 m × 1.0–1.5 m (very slow) | Compact, zigzag branching | Spring blossom (pale pink), autumn colour (orange-red), winter silhouette | Minimal — no regular pruning needed. Occasional thinning. | Naturally compact; will not outgrow the space. The twisted branches provide winter architecture. A classic small-garden tree seen in London front gardens. Tolerates clay. |
| 2 | **Amelanchier 'La Paloma'** (compact serviceberry) | 2.0–2.5 m × 1.0–1.5 m | Upright, multi-stemmed | White spring blossom, small berries (summer), vivid autumn colour (orange-red) | Minimal pruning. Remove suckers if multi-stemmed. | Bred specifically for small spaces. The upright habit keeps the footprint narrow. Four-season interest. Tolerates clay and partial shade. |
| 3 | **Magnolia stellata 'Royal Star'** (star magnolia) | 2.0–2.5 m × 1.5–2.0 m (very slow — 10+ years) | Rounded, compact | Stunning white star-shaped flowers (March–April) before leaves, subtle autumn colour | Minimal — no regular pruning. Mulch annually. Protect flowers from late frost if possible. | Extremely slow-growing, so will remain small for many years. The spring flowers are spectacular and very early. Prefers slightly acid soil — add ericaceous mulch on London clay. A prestige plant. |
| 4 | **Hydrangea paniculata 'Limelight'** (trained as standard) | 1.5–2.0 m × 0.8–1.0 m (on clear stem) | Clear stem, rounded head | Large lime-green to pink-white panicles (July–October), dried heads persist into winter | Annual prune in March (cut back to 2–3 buds). Feed and mulch. | Trained as a standard "lollipop," the clear stem keeps the ground free for bike access. The crown is at head height — no obstruction below. Very long flowering season. Completely hardy on clay. Quintessentially Bedford Park. |

### Recommendation

**Option 4 (Standard Hydrangea paniculata 'Limelight')** is the most practical choice — the clear stem maximises ground clearance for bikes, and the long flowering season (July–October) is unmatched. However, **Option 1 (Prunus incisa 'Kojo-no-mai')** offers the best four-season interest and the most characterful winter silhouette.

For a Bedford Park front garden, either would be excellent. Choose the Prunus for year-round architectural interest, or the standard Hydrangea for maximum flower power.

## 14. Final Recommended Planting Scheme

### Recommendation: **Scheme A — "Bedford Park Heritage"**

**Rationale:**

1. **Most appropriate to context.** Bedford Park is an Arts & Crafts conservation area. Scheme A's palette of hydrangeas, roses, lavender, and star jasmine is exactly what you see in the best-kept front gardens on the estate. It will look immediately "right."

2. **Best colour range.** The combination of pinks, blues, lavender, and white gives the most visual variety within a harmonious palette — critical in a small garden where there are few plants.

3. **Strong year-round interest.** Viburnum tinus flowers through winter, Sarcococca provides January scent, star jasmine is evergreen, and Prunus 'Kojo-no-mai' has winter architecture. Summer is the peak with roses, hydrangea, and lavender.

4. **Practical.** Lavender and Geranium 'Rozanne' are among the most reliable performers in London clay gardens. Star jasmine thrives on warm west-facing walls. Viburnum tinus is virtually indestructible.

5. **Moderate maintenance.** No box clipping. The main tasks are: deadhead roses (monthly in summer), trim lavender after flowering, cut back Geranium in autumn, annual feed/mulch.

### Suggested Modification

Consider substituting the **standard Hydrangea paniculata 'Limelight'** as the focal plant instead of Prunus incisa, if bike access is the primary concern. The clear stem provides better ground-level clearance. Alternatively, keep Prunus incisa and remove the lowest branches once established.
